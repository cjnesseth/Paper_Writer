\title{Capping the Capacity Market: A Supply Function Equilibrium Analysis of Price Controls in PJM}
\author{}
\date{\today}
\maketitle
\thispagestyle{empty}

\begin{abstract}
\singlespacing
In January 2025, Pennsylvania Governor Josh Shapiro negotiated a settlement
reshaping PJM's capacity market demand curve: a price cap at \$325/MW-day
UCAP and a price floor at \$175/MW-day UCAP for the 2026/27 and 2027/28
delivery years, projected to save consumers over \$21~billion. I evaluate
this intervention using a calibrated Supply Function Equilibrium model of
PJM's Base Residual Auction. The settlement cap is binding: under the
observed market structure with three collectively pivotal suppliers, the
model predicts clearing prices at the VRR Point~(a) cap (\$329/MW-day in
2026/27), exceeding the settlement cap by \$4--\$8/MW-day at the RTO level,
with larger gaps in constrained Locational Deliverability Areas. The settlement
suppresses prices but leaves the underlying price-cost margin unchanged. The
\$21~billion savings figure uses the unconstrained VRR Point~(a) price as
the counterfactual; a structural SFE benchmark implies a substantially smaller
two-year transfer at the RTO level, and the fixed-quantity assumption overstates
savings to the extent the cap depresses future entry and accelerates retirement.
The complaint's lead-time argument is partial: even at short auction horizons,
prices inform retirement deterrence, contracting, and forward investment
expectations. Transmission expansion (increasing the Capacity Emergency
Transfer Limit) reduces equilibrium prices organically, preserving the scarcity
signal while addressing the structural source of binding settlement outcomes.
\end{abstract}
