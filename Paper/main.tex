\title{Capping the Capacity Market: A Supply Function Equilibrium Analysis of Price Controls in PJM}
\author{}
\date{\today}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
In January 2025, Pennsylvania Governor Josh Shapiro negotiated a settlement
reshaping PJM's capacity market demand curve: a price cap at \$325/MW-day
UCAP and a price floor at \$175/MW-day UCAP for the 2026/27 and 2027/28
delivery years, projected to save consumers over \$21~billion. We evaluate
this intervention using a calibrated Supply Function Equilibrium model of
PJM's Base Residual Auction. Under the observed market structure --- three
pivotal suppliers with residual supply indices below unity --- equilibrium
prices reach the VRR Point~(a) cap in 2026/27 (\$329/MW-day) and exceed the
settlement cap by \$4--\$8/MW-day at the RTO level, with larger gaps in
constrained Locational Deliverability Areas. The settlement is therefore
binding but leaves the underlying markup structure nearly intact: the
price-cost margin falls by less than one percentage point (from 0.544
at the SFE equilibrium to 0.538 at the settlement cap). The \$21~billion
savings figure uses the unconstrained VRR Point~(a) price as the
counterfactual; our structural model provides an alternative estimate of
approximately \$612~million for the two-year period --- 35~times smaller ---
because the strategic equilibrium falls near the design ceiling rather than
far above the settlement cap. The complaint's lead-time argument is partial:
even at short lead times, prices inform retirement decisions, mobile resource
deployment, contracting, and investment expectations. Transmission expansion
(increasing the Capacity Emergency Transfer Limit) reduces equilibrium prices
organically by expanding the competitive fringe, preserving the scarcity
signal while addressing the structural source of high prices.
\end{abstract}
