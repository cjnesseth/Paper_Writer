\title{Price Suppression and Reliability Shortfall in PJM's Capacity Market}
\author{Christopher Nesseth}
\date{}
\maketitle
\thispagestyle{empty}

\begin{abstract}
\singlespacing
The January 2025 Shapiro settlement imposed a price cap of approximately \$325/MW-day and a floor of approximately \$175/MW-day on PJM’s 2026/27 and 2027/28 capacity auctions, with projected consumer savings above \$21~billion. This paper evaluates whether the settlement merely reduced capacity market payments or also suppressed the scarcity price signal in a tight market. I combine PJM auction data, Independent Market Monitor reports, and a calibrated symmetric supply function equilibrium framework to compare pre-cap auctions with the two settlement years. The capped auctions cleared at the operative cap and fell short of the reliability requirement by 0.23\% in 2026/27 and 4.7\% in 2027/28, with no comparable shortfall in prior sample auctions. The Monitor’s unrestricted demand counterfactual implies a two-year revenue difference of \$13.08~billion and roughly 9{,}500~MW of additional cleared capacity in 2027/28. The headline \$21~billion savings figure therefore conflates price suppression with reduced procurement. If the underlying scarcity was genuine, the settlement reduced consumer payments at the cost of weakening the investment signal in precisely the years when capacity prices were most informative.
\end{abstract}
