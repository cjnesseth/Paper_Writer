\title{Price Suppression and Reliability Shortfall in PJM's Capacity Market}
\author{}
\date{\today}
\maketitle
\thispagestyle{empty}

\begin{abstract}
\singlespacing
The January 2025 Shapiro settlement modified PJM's capacity-auction
demand curve to impose a price cap of approximately \$325 per
megawatt-day of unforced capacity (UCAP) and a price floor of
approximately \$175/MW-day on the 2026/27 and 2027/28 Base Residual
Auctions, with a projected consumer benefit of over \$21~billion.
Standard price-control theory predicts that a binding ceiling on a
tight market truncates both the price signal and the quantity
procured at the cap. I evaluate the connection by combining a
calibrated symmetric supply function equilibrium of PJM's seven
completed auctions with the Independent Market Monitor's
unrestricted-VRR counterfactual. The SFE confirms that under
IMM-reported concentration ($\mathrm{RSI}_3 < 1$) the unconstrained
equilibrium clears at the design-level Point~(a) cap and the
settlement cap binds. The cap-incidence panel shows that a quantity
shortfall relative to the reliability requirement emerged in both
capped years (0.23\% in 2026/27 and 4.7\% in 2027/28), with no
comparable shortfall in any prior auction in the sample. The IMM's
mechanical counterfactual implies a system-level two-year revenue
difference of \$13.08~billion and would have procured approximately
9{,}500~MW more capacity in 2027/28, against a Pennsylvania-stated
figure of approximately \$21~billion that mixes the price and
quantity channels. Under any reading consistent with real
underlying scarcity, the cap induces a quantity shortfall and
truncates the investment signal precisely in the years it would
have been most informative.
\end{abstract}
