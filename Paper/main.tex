\title{Capping the Capacity Market: A Supply Function Equilibrium Analysis of Price Controls in PJM}
\author{}
\date{\today}
\maketitle
\thispagestyle{empty}

\begin{abstract}
\singlespacing
In January 2025, Pennsylvania Governor Josh Shapiro negotiated a settlement
reshaping PJM's capacity market demand curve: a price cap at \$325 per
megawatt-day (MW-day) of unforced capacity (UCAP) and a price floor at
\$175/MW-day UCAP for the 2026/27 and 2027/28 delivery years, projected to
save consumers over \$21~billion. I evaluate this intervention using a
Supply Function Equilibrium (SFE) model calibrated to all seven completed
Base Residual Auctions (BRAs). Under the maintained assumption of $K \leq 3$
collectively pivotal suppliers, the model's equilibrium lies in an at-cap
regime in which the Holmberg boundary condition mechanically fixes the
clearing price at the Variable Resource Requirement (VRR) Point~(a) value,
so the 2026/27 and 2027/28 outcomes are consistent with the model's
structural regime rather than an independent forecast of the dollar price.
The substantive quantitative result is the binding gap between the VRR
Point~(a) value (\$329/MW-day in 2026/27) and the settlement cap
(\$325/MW-day)---\$4--\$8/MW-day at the Regional Transmission Organization
(RTO) level, with larger gaps in constrained Locational Deliverability Areas
(LDAs)---and the transition from at-cap to interior clearing between $K = 3$
and $K = 4$. Using the SFE rather than the unconstrained VRR Point~(a) as
the counterfactual, the implied two-year RTO-level revenue transfer is
approximately \$612~million, substantially smaller than the \$21~billion
figure. Because both 2026/27 and 2027/28 cleared below their reliability
requirements, however, the at-cap outcome is consistent with both the
strategic mechanism and physical scarcity, and the counterfactual magnitude
is sensitive to which Point~(a) represents the pre-settlement benchmark.
\end{abstract}
