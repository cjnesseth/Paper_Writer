\section{Calibration}
\label{sec:calibration}

\label{subsec:cal_data}

The calibration draws on four publicly available datasets produced by PJM
and the IMM.
PJM publishes VRR curve anchor points, Net CONE, CETL, and reliability
requirements for every LDA in advance of each BRA. I compiled planning
parameter files for delivery years 2021/22 through 2028/29, covering both
the three-point (old) and four-point (new) curve designs.
PJM releases clearing prices and quantities by LDA after each auction;
the sample covers seven completed auctions (2021/22 through 2027/28),
verified against PJM press releases.
Residual Supply Index values and participant counts are drawn from the
IMM's annual State of the Market reports (Volumes 2022 and 2025). RSI data
is available for a subset of LDAs and delivery years, as documented in
Table~\ref{tab:tps_results}.
Lead time is defined as the number of calendar months between the BRA
settlement date and the first day of the delivery year
(Table~\ref{tab:bra_timeline}).
The four datasets are joined at the LDA--delivery-year level to produce a
master panel with 110 observations spanning 17 LDAs and seven delivery years
(2021/22 through 2027/28).

\label{subsec:cal_vrr}

Table~\ref{tab:vrr_params} reports the VRR curve parameters for the RTO
under both the old and new designs for the six delivery years with complete
BRA data. Under the old design, Point~(a) equals $1.5 \times \text{Net CONE}$
and Point~(b) equals $0.75 \times \text{Net CONE}$ by construction, so the
ratio $p_{a} / p_{b} = 2$ is exact for all old-design years except 2025/26,
where a transitional administrative cap produced a Point~(a) price (\$451.61)
above the standard $1.5 \times \text{Net CONE}$ multiple (\$343.22), because
VRR anchor points are fixed at planning-parameter publication and may diverge
from the final Net CONE. I use the published anchor points as the realized VRR
parameters throughout.
Under the new design, Points~(b) and~(c) equal the explicit floor price of
approximately \$177--\$179 per MW-day, and Point~(a) is calibrated by FERC
Order ER25-1357. The new-design Point~(a) prices (\$329.17 for 2026/27,
\$333.44 for 2027/28) do not equal simple Net CONE multiples because they
reflect the settlement implementation and are fixed from planning parameters
published before each auction. The settlement's flat-top at \$325/MW-day
creates binding gaps of \$4.17 and \$8.44 respectively below these
Point~(a) values.

The panel encompasses 110 LDA--delivery-year cells with observed clearing
prices across 17 LDAs. All seven completed BRA years are used in the
analysis: five old-design years (2021/22 through 2025/26) spanning clearing
prices from \$28.92 to \$269.92/MW-day, and two new-design years (2026/27
and 2027/28) that both cleared at exactly the Point~(a) cap.

\label{subsec:cal_market_structure}

Capacity market concentration in PJM is high by any standard measure. The PJM
IMM reports LDA-level Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) values in its annual
State of the Market (SotM) reports. In every delivery year covered by the
sample, the RTO-level HHI exceeds 2{,}500 and most constrained LDAs exceed
4{,}000, thresholds the Department of Justice classifies as
highly concentrated markets under standard antitrust benchmarks.

The key calibration input is the number of strategic sellers, $K$. I use the
Residual Supply Index with threshold three (RSI$_3$) as the calibration
criterion: RSI$_3 < 1$ means that the three largest sellers collectively hold
pivotal capacity, i.e., no competitive outcome is supportable without their
participation. In all seven delivery years, the RTO-level RSI$_3$ falls below
unity (ranging from 0.62 to 0.73; Table~\ref{tab:tps_results}),
consistent with at most three large sellers being jointly pivotal. The
2026/27 at-cap clearing outcome is also consistent with $K \leq 3$: the
model predicts interior clearing for four or more symmetric sellers in that
year (Section~\ref{subsec:res_concentration}), so the observed at-cap
outcome aligns with a low effective strategic-seller count under the
maintained model. The IMM TPS test confirms individual
pivotality for all three largest sellers in every constrained LDA, supporting
$K = 3$ over $K = 2$ as the baseline. I therefore set $K = 3$ as the
baseline strategic count, and examine sensitivity to this assumption via
the $K$ comparative static in Section~\ref{subsec:res_concentration}. Because RSI data is available only at the RTO level
for most delivery years, I impose the same $K = 3$ structure on each LDA
calibration and allow cross-LDA variation to enter through the VRR parameters
and CETL-based fringe capacity. This homogeneity across LDAs is a known
limitation. Where the IMM reports $\text{RSI}_1 < 1$ (e.g., BGE, DEOK in
certain years), a single supplier is pivotal and the effective $K$ is closer
to one; the symmetric $K = 3$ calibration there understates local market
power. Conversely, in LDAs with $\text{RSI}_3 > 1$, three suppliers are not
jointly pivotal and $K = 3$ overstates concentration. LDA-level Lerner
indices should therefore be read as applying a common structural assumption
to heterogeneous local conditions rather than as LDA-specific estimates.

\label{subsec:cal_cost}

The ACR for each strategic seller is set to
$c = \$150$ per MW-day, the combined-cycle sector average reported in the 2025
PJM IMM State of the Market \citep[Table~7-38, \$149.32/MW-day, rounded]{MonitoringAnalytics2025_sotm}.
Combined-cycle units represent the marginal capacity technology in PJM's
current resource mix, and the IMM identifies the combined-cycle ACR as the
relevant benchmark for capacity-offer mitigation under the TPS test
\citep{MonitoringAnalytics2025_sotm, MonitoringAnalytics2025_bra2627}. The symmetric-firm assumption sets all $K = 3$
sellers to the same $c$, and heterogeneity in costs and capacity sizes is
abstracted away. It is important to note that the ACR is a proxy for
opportunity cost, not its direct measure. The true avoidable cost of
offering a megawatt of capacity depends on projected energy-market revenues,
fuel hedge positions, maintenance schedules, and the firm's financial
structure, none of which are publicly observable at the portfolio level.
The sector-average combined-cycle ACR therefore understates cost
heterogeneity across strategic sellers. The sensitivity of equilibrium
markups to this approximation is examined directly in
Table~\ref{tab:cost_sensitivity}, which varies $c$ over
\$100--\$200 per MW-day at $K = 3$ and shows that the qualitative
settlement-binding finding is robust across this range. The range is also
informative as a bracketing device: applying a uniform ACR of \$100/MW-day
to all three strategic sellers yields an upper Lerner bound of 0.70 in the
2026/27 at-cap equilibrium, while a uniform ACR of \$200/MW-day yields a
lower bound of 0.39. Heterogeneous-cost configurations whose sector-average
ACR is within this range need not produce a Lerner index strictly between
these symmetric bounds---the symmetric SFE is not a proven envelope---but
the bracketing analysis establishes that the central Lerner estimate of
0.54 at $c = \$150$ is not an artifact of a narrow assumed cost level.

Fringe supply is modeled as an inelastic block offered at $c$: any capacity
not controlled by the $K$ strategic sellers is assumed to offer at cost.
At the RTO level, fringe capacity is set to total installed capacity minus the
capacity attributed to the three largest sellers. This residual is scaled with
$\gamma$ to explore fringe-size sensitivity. For individual LDA calibrations,
CETL serves as the effective fringe
capacity, since CETL measures the maximum deliverable import from the rest of
PJM into the LDA. LDA-level CETL values by delivery year are drawn directly
from the PJM planning parameter files described in
Section~\ref{subsec:cal_data}.

\label{subsec:cal_years}

I calibrate and solve the model for all seven completed BRA delivery years
(2021/22 through 2027/28), spanning both VRR design regimes and the full
range of observed market conditions. Five auctions (2021/22 through 2025/26)
were conducted under the old three-point VRR design; two (2026/27 and
2027/28) under the new four-point design with an explicit price floor. The
SFE is a static game in which sellers submit supply functions against the
VRR curve; the auction mechanism is structurally identical across all seven
years regardless of lead time, so no year is excluded on timing grounds.
