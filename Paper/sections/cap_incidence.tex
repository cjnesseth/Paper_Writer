\section{Empirical Results}
\label{sec:cap_incidence}

This section documents which administrative constraint was binding in
each of PJM's seven completed BRAs and shows that a
quantity shortfall against the reliability requirement emerged
precisely when the Shapiro settlement cap began to bind. The textbook
prediction of Section~\ref{subsec:theory_ceiling}, that a binding
ceiling on a tight market produces a quantity shortfall against the
planning target, is matched by the timing of the observed shortfall in
PJM.

Three regimes are visible in Table~\ref{tab:cap_incidence},
distinguished by the joint pattern of clearing price, reserve excess
relative to the Installed Reserve Margin (IRM), and IMM-applied
mitigation activity.

The first regime, covering 2021/22 through 2024/25, is one of comfortable
supply margins and cost-anchored clearing. Reserve excess IRM was
strongly positive in 2023/24 and 2024/25 (+5{,}687~MW and +3{,}278~MW),
with data unavailable for 2021/22 and 2022/23. Clearing prices ranged
from \$29 to \$140/MW-day, well below both the sector average Net ACR
and the design-level VRR Point~(a) cap of \$391--\$482/MW-day. TPS
mitigation was applied to 0--7\% of resources, but with the marginal
cleared offer well below any active cap, mitigation operative for a
minority of supra-marginal resources did not pin the clearing outcome.

The second regime, in 2025/26, is a transition. Reserve excess IRM
turned slightly negative for the first time (\textminus205~MW), the
clearing price jumped from \$29 to \$270/MW-day, and TPS mitigation
rose to 5.5\% of resources. \$270 still falls well below the design
Point~(a) of \$452, so no administrative cap was binding the clearing.
2025/26 is the auction in which the underlying supply and demand balance
tightened without any cap yet binding the headline outcome,
and the auction still procured adequate capacity.

The third regime, 2026/27 and 2027/28, is one of cap-binding
clearing under negative reserve excess. Both auctions cleared at the
new-design VRR Point~(a) value, which was the agreed maximum price under the Shapiro/PJM settlement. Reserve excess IRM was
\textminus492~MW in 2026/27 and \textminus6{,}517~MW in 2027/28, and
the IMM declared both auctions "not competitive"
\citep{MonitoringAnalytics2025_sotm}. The drop in TPS mitigation
application from 6.3\% in 2026/27 to 1.0\% in 2027/28 fits a story in
which the settlement cap moved sufficiently close to the underlying
offer distribution that fewer individual offers exceeded their
unit-specific Net ACR caps. The shortfall relative to the reliability
requirement emerged in the same years that the cap became
binding.

The cap-incidence panel offers a direct answer to a recurring question
about the post-settlement auctions. Did seller behavior change between
the slack and tight regimes, or did the binding administrative
constraint change while the underlying competitive process remained
similar? The data favor the latter interpretation. In slack years,
clearings of \$29--\$140/MW-day fell well below any plausible
unconstrained strategic price, and cost-anchored offers and TPS
mitigation held the headline price. In the cap-binding years,
clearing equaled the cap by construction in any model in which the
at-cap regime applies. The dramatic price changes between regimes
therefore reflect a change in which administrative
constraint binds, not a structural shift in conduct. The independent
evidence that this is not a circular reading is the \emph{timing} of
the reserve margin flip. Reserve excess IRM turned negative in the
2025/26 transition auction, one year before the cap took effect, and
stayed negative in the two cap-binding years that followed.
