\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Capacity markets are designed to solve an investment problem: generators will
not commit capital to build new capacity if energy-market revenues alone are
insufficient to cover fixed costs. But if the market is thin --- a small
number of sellers hold pivotal capacity --- the same mechanism that
compensates investment can generate substantial market power rents. This paper
asks how large those rents are in PJM's Reliability Pricing Model and what
market-design choices govern their magnitude.

We answer the question using a calibrated Supply Function Equilibrium model.
Sellers simultaneously choose quantity-contingent supply functions; the
equilibrium is selected by Holmberg's boundary condition, which pins down
a unique SFE when capacity constraints are binding. We calibrate the model to
PJM's VRR demand curve, three-pivotal-supplier market structure, and
combined-cycle ACR of \$150/MW-day using publicly available planning parameters
and IMM State of the Market data for delivery years 2021/22 through 2027/28.

The baseline results are striking. With $K = 3$ strategic sellers --- the
number identified by RSI$_3 < 1$ in every benchmark year --- the model
predicts Lerner indices of 0.54--0.65, implying equilibrium prices 2.2--2.9
times the competitive level. The strongest validation comes from 2026/27:
the model selects $p^* = \$329.17$/MW-day without tuning, matching the
observed at-cap clearing price exactly. This at-cap outcome is informative ---
it rules out effective competition among four or more symmetric sellers ---
and confirms that the transition to the new four-point VRR design did not
eliminate strategic pricing incentives.

The comparative statics identify two structural levers for reducing market
power. First, concentration: each additional strategic seller beyond three
substantially reduces markups, and $K \geq 7$ is near-competitive
($L < 0.02$). Entry promotion policies that credibly reduce the pivotal
supplier count have the largest marginal impact. Second, the VRR demand slope:
steepening the curve by a factor of two reduces interior-year Lerner values
by 20--30 percentage points. The new VRR design moves in this direction
but simultaneously raises the price floor, limiting the markup reduction for
at-cap outcomes. At the LDA level, import penetration --- measured by
CETL relative to the reliability requirement --- is the dominant
cross-sectional predictor of market power: each ten-percentage-point
increase in import penetration reduces the Lerner index by approximately
five percentage points.

The policy punchline is twofold. Mitigation rules based on ACR-level offer
caps are necessary but insufficient: they address price withholding but not
quantity withholding, and the binary TPS test understates the magnitude of
markups we estimate. Transmission investment that expands CETL in constrained
LDAs would address market power at its structural root by increasing effective
competition without changing auction rules.

Several directions for future work are suggested by the model's limitations.
An asymmetric-firm SFE with heterogeneous capacities and costs would
characterize how markup dispersion interacts with the TPS screen. A dynamic
version with endogenous entry and exit would connect short-run strategic
pricing to long-run capacity adequacy. Most ambitiously, an empirical
structural model that recovers firm-level cost parameters from observed
offer data would allow direct welfare calculation and policy counterfactuals.
The calibrated SFE results in this paper provide a benchmark for assessing
whether any of these extensions materially change the central conclusion:
market power in PJM's capacity auctions is large, quantifiable, and sensitive
to market-design choices that policymakers directly control.
