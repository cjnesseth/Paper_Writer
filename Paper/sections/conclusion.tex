\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Standard price-control theory predicts that a binding ceiling on a
tight market truncates the price signal and produces a quantity
shortfall against the planning target. PJM's two capped delivery
years, 2026/27 and 2027/28, show both effects. Clearing prices were
held at the new-design Point~(a) value, and cleared capacity fell
short of the reliability requirement by 314~MW and 6{,}623~MW
respectively (Section~\ref{sec:case_studies}). The shortfall
emerged in the same years the cap became binding
(Section~\ref{sec:cap_incidence}), with no comparable shortfall in
any prior auction in the sample. The textbook prediction and the
data line up.

The Pennsylvania-stated savings figure of approximately
\$21~billion compares actual revenue under the settlement-modified
VRR to a counterfactual price "over \$500/MW-day"
\citep{Shapiro2025_press_release} applied to the capped quantity, and
so mixes the price and quantity channels. The IMM's mechanical
unrestricted-VRR counterfactual, which holds offers fixed and
re-clears against the pre-settlement demand curve, implies two-year
revenues of \$45.62~billion against actual \$32.53~billion. The
system-level difference is \$13.08~billion, and under the same
scenario the auction would have procured approximately 9{,}500~MW
more capacity in 2027/28 (Table~\ref{tab:capped_revenue}). Treating
the difference as "savings" conflates a price reduction with a
quantity rationing shortfall. Under the cap, consumers paid less,
and in 2027/28 particularly the auction procured less than the
reliability target.

Lead time compression amplifies the investment signal cost. The
2025/26, 2026/27, and 2027/28 BRAs were held with 10 to 18 months of
lead time against a three-year forward design
(Table~\ref{tab:bra_timeline}). A 10-month horizon is too short for
greenfield gas generator construction, given documented permitting,
interconnection-queue, and equipment-procurement timelines for new
thermal entry \citep{Brattle2025_cone, PJM2026_shortfall_report},
but may still matter for shorter-lead decisions involving demand
response, battery storage, capacity upgrades to existing plants,
and retirement deferral, all of which are eligible capacity resource
types under PJM's tariff \citep{PJM_Manual18}. Auction prices also
inform retirement deferral decisions on the same timescale
\citep{Cramton2005_capacity}. A two-year cap layered on a
tight market therefore disrupts the retirement and short-deployment
signal precisely in the years when it would have been most
informative, and the new four-point VRR design potentially recovers
some of that signal in subsequent auctions
\citep{FERC2025_ER25-1357}.

The welfare reading depends on whether the underlying scarcity is
real or strategic. If sellers were exercising market power and the
unconstrained equilibrium reflected rents above efficient scarcity
prices, the cap would be welfare improving. If the underlying
scarcity is real, driven by load growth and supply tightening that
the IMM and PJM both attribute to forecast data-center demand,
thermal retirements, queue transition, and supply-chain friction
\citep{MonitoringAnalytics2025_sotm, PJM2026_shortfall_report}, the
cap induces a quantity shortage in years when capacity adequacy is
most needed and cuts off the very price signal the capacity market
exists to send.
The cap-incidence panel of Section~\ref{sec:cap_incidence} favors
that reading when paired with PJM's and the IMM's attribution of
recent capacity-market stress to load growth, retirements, queue
transition, and supply-chain friction
\citep{MonitoringAnalytics2025_sotm, PJM2026_shortfall_report}. The
declared "not competitive" status of both auctions cuts the other
way, however, and a full welfare conclusion is not available without
unit-level offer data.

The natural next step for further research is to separate scarcity from conduct more directly. Unit-level offer data would allow an asymmetric-firm SFE that recovers seller-specific costs and markups, while a dynamic capacity adequacy model could connect capped revenues to entry, retirement, demand response, and storage participation. That would extend the present analysis from a cap-incidence exercise to a fuller estimate of the settlement’s long-run resource adequacy consequences.
