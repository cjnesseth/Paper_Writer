\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Standard price-control theory predicts that a binding ceiling on a
tight market truncates the price signal and produces a quantity
shortfall against the planning target. PJM's two capped delivery
years, 2026/27 and 2027/28, show both effects: clearing prices pinned
at the new-design Point~(a) value and cleared capacity short of the
reliability requirement by 314~MW and 6{,}623~MW, respectively
(Section~\ref{sec:case_studies}). The shortfall emerged in the same
years the cap became binding (Section~\ref{sec:cap_incidence}), with
no comparable shortfall in any prior auction in the sample. The
textbook prediction and the empirical realization line up.

The Pennsylvania-stated savings figure of approximately
\$21~billion compares actual revenue under the settlement-modified
VRR to a counterfactual price ``over \$500/MW-day''
\citep{Shapiro2025_press_release} applied to the capped quantity, and
so mixes the price and quantity channels. The IMM's mechanical
unrestricted-VRR counterfactual, which holds offers fixed and
re-clears against the pre-settlement demand curve, implies two-year
revenues of \$45.62~billion against actual \$32.53~billion---a
system-level difference of \$13.08~billion---and would have procured
approximately 9{,}500~MW more capacity in 2027/28
(Table~\ref{tab:capped_revenue}). Treating the difference as
``savings'' conflates a price reduction with a quantity-rationing
shortfall: under the cap, consumers paid less, and---in 2027/28
particularly---the auction procured less than the reliability target.

Lead-time compression amplifies the investment-signal cost. The
2025/26, 2026/27, and 2027/28 BRAs were held with 10--18 months of
lead time against a canonical three-year forward design
(Table~\ref{tab:bra_timeline}). A 10-month horizon is too short for
greenfield gas but non-trivial for demand response, battery storage,
and capacity upgrades to existing plants; auction prices inform
retirement-deferral decisions on the same timescale
\citep{Cramton2005_capacity}. A two-year cap during a tight-supply
episode therefore disrupts the retirement and short-deployment
signal precisely in the years when it would have been most
informative, with the new four-point VRR design potentially
recovering some of that signal in subsequent auctions
\citep{FERC2025_ER25-1357}.

The welfare reading depends on whether the underlying scarcity is
real or strategic. If sellers were exercising market power and the
unconstrained equilibrium reflected rents above efficient scarcity
prices, the cap is unambiguously welfare-improving. If the underlying
scarcity is real---driven by load growth and supply tightening, which
the IMM attributes to data-center demand
\citep{MonitoringAnalytics2025_sotm}---the cap induces a
quantity shortage in years when capacity adequacy is most needed and
truncates the very price signal the capacity market exists to send.
The cap-incidence panel of Section~\ref{sec:cap_incidence}, the
declared ``not competitive'' status of both auctions, and the
realization of the textbook ceiling-induced shortfall together favor
the latter reading.

The paper is restricted to evaluating the connection between the
standard price-control prediction and the PJM evidence. Two natural
extensions for distinguishing scarcity from conduct quantitatively
are an asymmetric-firm SFE that recovers seller-specific costs from
offer data, and a dynamic capacity-adequacy model connecting markups
to entry and retirement decisions.
