\section{Institutional Background}
\label{sec:institutional}

\label{subsec:inst_primitives}

PJM Interconnection operates the largest competitive wholesale electricity
market in North America, coordinating approximately 201{,}000 megawatts of
installed generating capacity across thirteen states and the District of
Columbia. Its capacity market, the Reliability Pricing Model (RPM), was
introduced in 2007 to procure forward commitments to be available to
produce energy during a future delivery year \citep{Bowring2013_pjm,
PJM_Manual18, Pfeifenberger2011_rpm}. A capacity commitment is distinct
from the energy itself, which is sold separately in PJM's day-ahead and
real-time markets: the capacity payment compensates a resource for
standing ready, while energy payments compensate it for actual generation
when called upon.

The market exists to address the ``missing-money'' problem. Regulatory
price caps in the wholesale energy market suppress the scarcity rents
that would otherwise finance investment in peaking generation, leaving
energy revenues insufficient to recover the fixed costs of new entry
\citep{Joskow2007_capacity, Joskow2008_capacity_payments,
Cramton2005_capacity}. RPM fills the gap with a separate forward
instrument that pays for availability and so completes the revenue
stream needed for resource adequacy.

The primary procurement mechanism is the Base Residual Auction (BRA),
held approximately three years before the start of the delivery year
that the auction procures for. Sellers submit offers, PJM clears the
auction at a uniform price, and cleared resources receive that clearing
price, in dollars per megawatt-day, for every day of the twelve-month
delivery year, paid out as monthly capacity revenue. The 2026/27
delivery year, for example, was procured in the BRA held in July 2025;
the clearing price set in that auction governs payments to cleared
resources from June 2026 through May 2027. Recent BRAs have run on
compressed lead times relative to the canonical three-year forward
design (Table~\ref{tab:bra_timeline}); the implications of this
compression for entry response and for the Shapiro complaint are
discussed below.

Capacity is denominated in unforced capacity (UCAP) terms rather than
nameplate installed capacity (ICAP). Each resource's accreditation
ratio is the fraction of nameplate capacity it is expected to deliver
during system stress, accounting for forced outages and, more recently,
Effective Load Carrying Capability (ELCC) adjustments for intermittent
and energy-limited resources \citep{PJM_Manual18, PJM_Manual20A}. The
reference dual-fuel combustion turbine used in PJM's planning
parameters has an accreditation of approximately 79\%, so a 100~MW
nameplate unit clears as 79~MW of UCAP and is paid the clearing price
on those 79~MW. Throughout the paper, prices and quantities are stated
in UCAP terms unless noted otherwise.

\label{subsec:inst_participants}

The bidders in a BRA are owners of capacity resources: existing
generators (the dominant category by both count and cleared MW),
demand response providers, planned new generation, and behind-the-meter
distributed resources. Multi-unit ownership is the norm, with large
generation owners typically participating with portfolios of dozens to
hundreds of resources spread across multiple Locational Deliverability
Areas (LDAs). Offers are submitted on a per-resource basis rather than
at the firm level, so a single firm enters the auction as a collection
of resource-level offers rather than as a single bidder with a
portfolio supply schedule. There is no buy-side bidding in the
classical sense; PJM acts as the administrative buyer on behalf of
Load Serving Entities (LSEs), submitting demand for the RTO and for
each LDA through the Variable Resource Requirement (VRR) curve
described below.

A resource-level offer is a sealed price-quantity pair, denominated in
dollars per megawatt-day of UCAP, that specifies the minimum payment at
which the resource is willing to commit. Conceptually the offer is a
going-forward cost: the per-MW payment the resource needs above
expected energy and ancillary-service revenues to remain available
during the delivery year. For an existing resource this is a short-run
avoidable cost (operations, maintenance, and forgone alternative use);
for new entry it is the annualized fixed cost net of expected energy
revenues. The PJM Independent Market Monitor (IMM) computes a
resource-specific Net Avoidable Cost Rate (Net ACR) that serves both
as a default offer cap under mitigation and as the cost benchmark
referenced in the calibration of Section~\ref{sec:calibration}
\citep{MonitoringAnalytics2025_sotm}.

A cleared resource takes on a Capacity Performance obligation: it must
be available to produce energy when PJM declares Performance Assessment
Hours during the delivery year, and non-performance triggers a charge
calibrated to a multiple of Net CONE \citep{PJM_Manual18}. The
performance obligation completes the meaning of the capacity commitment
and ties the forward payment to delivery-year availability. The penalty
mechanics are not modeled in this paper; the SFE setup in
Section~\ref{sec:model} treats committed capacity as delivered.

\label{subsec:inst_clearing}

Clearing is a one-sided sealed-bid uniform-price auction. PJM aggregates
the resource-level offers in each LDA into an upward-stepping supply
schedule and intersects it with the LDA's VRR demand curve; the
intersection price is the LDA clearing price, and every resource whose
offer lies at or below that price clears its full offered quantity at
the uniform clearing price. Clearing is solved jointly across the RTO
and the constrained sub-LDAs subject to Capacity Emergency Transfer
Limit (CETL) constraints that bound the imports each LDA can receive
from the rest of the system \citep{PJM_Manual20A}. When a CETL
constraint binds, the LDA's clearing price separates from the RTO
price and the constrained LDA pays a locational premium.

In auction-theoretic terms, the BRA is a one-sided, sealed-bid,
uniform-price, multi-unit, multi-product mechanism with a publicly
known administrative demand schedule. The multi-unit dimension reflects
each seller's portfolio of resources within an LDA; the multi-product
dimension reflects the joint clearing across LDAs subject to CETL. The
publicly known demand curve, the VRR, is the primitive on which the
supply function equilibrium analysis in Sections~\ref{sec:model}
and~\ref{sec:results} rests, and it is the object that the 2025 Shapiro
settlement modified.

The empirical panel covers the RTO and sixteen sub-LDAs across delivery
years 2021/22 through 2027/28.

The VRR curve is the administrative demand schedule presented to sellers in
each BRA \citep{PJM_Manual18}. Under the old design (used for delivery years
2021/22 through 2025/26), it is defined by three anchor points: Point~(a), the
price cap at $1.5 \times \text{Net CONE}$, Point~(b) at
$0.75 \times \text{Net CONE}$ at the reliability requirement, and Point~(c)
at zero price above the reliability requirement \citep{Bowring2013_pjm,
Pfeifenberger2011_rpm}. The Net Cost of New Entry (Net CONE) is the annualized cost of a reference
generating unit, net of projected energy and ancillary service revenues,
computed independently for each LDA \citep{PJM_Manual18, PJM_Manual20A}.
FERC approved a restructured VRR curve design in 2025 \citep{FERC2025_ER25-1357},
taking effect with the 2026/27 delivery year. The redesign added a fourth
anchor point, creating an explicit price floor at approximately \$177--\$179
per MW-day and eliminating the collapse to zero price for surplus capacity.

PJM's primary market power mitigation mechanism is the Three Pivotal Supplier
(TPS) test. Supplier $i$ is pivotal in LDA $\ell$ if removing $i$'s
capacity would cause aggregate supply to fall short of the reliability
requirement. The Residual Supply Index for $k$ suppliers is defined as
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{RSI}_{k}^{\ell} \;=\;
  \frac{\text{Total capacity in LDA }\ell
        \;-\; \text{Capacity of top-}k\text{ sellers}}
       {\text{Reliability requirement for LDA }\ell}.
  \label{eq:rsi}
\end{equation}
Mitigation is triggered when $\mathrm{RSI}_1 \leq 1$ or $\mathrm{RSI}_3 \leq 1$,
capping affected suppliers' offers at their Net Avoidable Cost Rate (ACR)
\citep{Bowring2013_pjm, MonitoringAnalytics2025_sotm}.

Table~\ref{tab:tps_results} summarizes RTO-level TPS test outcomes for the six
delivery years with complete IMM data. $\mathrm{RSI}_3$ is below 1 in every
year, ranging from 0.62 to 0.73, so the mitigation threshold is met throughout
the sample and concentration is persistent. The IMM also publishes
LDA-level RSI values in its annual State of the Market reports; the
cross-sectional snapshots corroborate this RTO pattern and document
individual pivotality in several small constrained LDAs
\citep{MonitoringAnalytics2025_sotm}.

\label{subsec:inst_shapiro}

The high BRA clearing prices of 2025/26 and the anticipation of similarly
elevated prices in subsequent auctions triggered political intervention
that directly modified the demand curve for two delivery years. On December
30, 2024, the Office of Governor Josh Shapiro filed a complaint against
PJM's tariff with the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission under Section
206 of the Federal Power Act. The complaint's legal theory was that the
VRR Point~(a) price cap was set too high given lower expectations for
new entry in the near term \citep{PJM2025_settlement_slides}. Because
compressed auction lead times (Table~\ref{tab:bra_timeline}) limited
the ability of new generation to respond to high prices within upcoming
delivery periods, the complaint argued that high prices would not produce
efficient entry and instead represented an unjust and unreasonable rate.
The proposed remedy was to lower the cap to $1.5 \times \text{Net CONE}$
and remove reliance on Gross CONE in the Point~(a) calculation.

Following confidential settlement discussions, PJM and the Governor's
office announced an agreement in principle on January 28, 2025
\citep{Shapiro2025_press_release}. The settlement was presented to the
PJM Markets Committee on February 7, 2025, and PJM
indicated a target filing date of February 14, 2025, with the 2026/27
BRA scheduled for July 2025 and the 2027/28 BRA for December 2025
\citep{PJM2025_settlement_slides}.
The agreement imposes a price band on the demand curves for both delivery
years: a price cap at approximately \$325/MW-day UCAP (Unforced Capacity),
equivalent to \$256.75/MW-day in Installed Capacity (ICAP) terms at the assumed 79\%
accreditation of the dual-fuel combustion turbine reference unit, and a
price floor at approximately \$175/MW-day UCAP (\$138.25/MW-day ICAP),
below which all available capacity clears regardless of the VRR slope. The settlement cap is denoted as $\bar{p}_S \approx \$325$/MW-day (UCAP) and the floor as $\bar{p}_f \approx \$175$/MW-day (UCAP) to distinguish them from the existing VRR Point~(a) cap $\bar{p}$. Both values are indexed to
the accreditation of the reference unit and may differ slightly from these
round numbers when final Effective Load Carrying Capability (ELCC) values are determined.

The settlement does not impose an external price constraint on an otherwise
unchanged auction. Instead, it modifies the shape of the VRR demand curve
itself \citep{PJM2025_settlement_slides}. At prices above $\bar{p}_S$, the demand
curve is flat (no capacity with offers above the cap can clear). Between $\bar{p}_S$
and $\bar{p}_f$, the existing VRR sloped segment applies.
At prices at or below $\bar{p}_f$, the demand curve extends
horizontally to clear all available capacity at the floor price. This mechanism applies
identically to the RTO demand curve and to every LDA demand curve. Clearing results continue to respect CETL transmission limitations \citep{PJM2025_settlement_slides, FERC2025_ER25-1357}.
The pre-existing Point~(a) cap $\bar{p}$ is calibrated to investment
incentives (approximately $1.5 \times \text{Net CONE}$), while the settlement cap
$\bar{p}_S$ is lower and is motivated by near-term consumer cost concerns.
Table~\ref{tab:bra_timeline} shows that $\bar{p} = \$329.17$/MW-day in
2026/27, only \$4.17 above $\bar{p}_S = \$325$/MW-day; the gap is larger
in constrained LDAs where local Point~(a) caps substantially exceeded
\$325 in prior years.
