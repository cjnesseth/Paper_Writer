\section{Institutional Background}
\label{sec:institutional}

PJM Interconnection operates the largest competitive wholesale
electricity market in North America, coordinating about 201{,}000~MW
of installed generating capacity across thirteen states and the
District of Columbia. Its capacity market, the Reliability Pricing
Model (RPM), was introduced in 2007 to procure forward commitments
to be available during a future delivery year \citep{Bowring2013_pjm,
PJM_Manual18, Pfeifenberger2011_rpm}. The market exists to address
the ``missing-money'' problem: regulatory price caps in the
wholesale energy market suppress the scarcity rents that would
otherwise finance investment in peaking generation, leaving energy
revenues insufficient to recover fixed costs of new entry
\citep{Joskow2007_capacity, Joskow2008_capacity_payments,
Cramton2005_capacity}. RPM fills the gap with a forward instrument
that pays for availability and so completes the revenue stream
needed for resource adequacy.

The primary procurement mechanism is the Base Residual Auction
(BRA), a one-sided sealed-bid uniform-price auction held
approximately three years before the start of the delivery year it
procures. PJM acts as the administrative buyer through a publicly
announced demand schedule (the Variable Resource Requirement, or
VRR, curve) and aggregates resource-level offers into an
upward-stepping supply schedule. Capacity is denominated in unforced
capacity (UCAP) terms rather than nameplate installed capacity
(ICAP). Each resource's accreditation ratio reflects the fraction
of nameplate capacity expected to deliver during system stress,
with forced-outage adjustments for thermal resources and Effective
Load Carrying Capability (ELCC) adjustments for intermittent and
energy-limited resources \citep{PJM_Manual18, PJM_Manual20A}. The
reference dual-fuel combustion turbine used in PJM's planning
parameters has an accreditation of approximately 79\%, so a 100~MW
nameplate unit clears as 79~MW of UCAP and is paid the clearing
price on those 79~MW. Clearing is solved jointly across the RTO and
constrained sub-LDAs subject to Capacity Emergency Transfer Limit
(CETL) constraints, which bound the imports each LDA can receive
from the rest of the system; when a CETL constraint binds, the
LDA's clearing price separates from the RTO price and the
constrained LDA pays a locational premium \citep{PJM_Manual20A}.

A cleared resource receives the LDA's uniform clearing price, in
dollars per MW-day of UCAP, for every day of the twelve-month
delivery year, settled monthly. A 200~MW UCAP cleared resource at a
\$300/MW-day clearing price thus receives roughly \$1.8~million per
month in capacity revenue. The cleared resource also takes on a
Capacity Performance (CP) obligation: it must be available to
produce energy when PJM declares Performance Assessment Hours
during the delivery year, typically tight intervals when reserves
are low \citep{PJM_Manual18}. Failure to deliver during a
Performance Assessment Hour triggers a non-performance charge
calibrated to a multiple of Net CONE, and over-performance during
the same hours earns a bonus payment funded from the
non-performance charges. The CP framework is intended to align
capacity payments with delivery-year availability rather than mere
ownership of nameplate capacity. The penalty mechanics themselves
are not modeled in this paper; the SFE setup of
Section~\ref{sec:theory} treats committed capacity as delivered.

Resources whose offers do not clear in the BRA have several
alternatives. PJM conducts three Incremental Auctions (1st, 2nd,
and 3rd IA) approximately twenty, ten, and four months before the
delivery year, in which capacity needs that change after the BRA---
driven by load-forecast revisions, planned-resource cancellations,
or replacement-capacity transactions---are procured or released; a
resource that did not clear the BRA can re-offer in any IA.
Outside RPM, a load-serving entity may opt out of PJM's centralized
procurement under the Fixed Resource Requirement (FRR) Alternative,
securing capacity bilaterally from generators with which it
contracts directly; FRR resources and load are removed from the BRA
and bilateral capacity arrangements substitute for centralized
clearing. A resource may also forgo capacity commitment for a
delivery year and seek revenue solely from the energy and
ancillary-service markets, accepting the lost capacity payment in
exchange for operational flexibility. Finally, an existing resource
contemplating retirement may file a Notice of Deactivation; if PJM
determines that retirement would create a reliability concern that
cannot be remedied through transmission upgrades or substitute
resources, the unit may be designated Reliability Must-Run (RMR)
and continue operating under a cost-of-service agreement at a
regulated rate distinct from the BRA clearing price.

The VRR curve is the administrative demand schedule presented to
sellers in each BRA \citep{PJM_Manual18}. Under the old three-point
design (delivery years 2021/22 through 2025/26) it is parameterized
by Point~(a), the price cap at $1.5 \times \text{Net CONE}$,
Point~(b) at $0.75 \times \text{Net CONE}$ at the reliability
requirement, and Point~(c) at zero price above the reliability
requirement \citep{Bowring2013_pjm, Pfeifenberger2011_rpm}. The
Net Cost of New Entry (Net CONE) is the annualized cost of a
reference generating unit, net of projected energy and
ancillary-service revenues, computed independently for each LDA
\citep{PJM_Manual18, PJM_Manual20A}. FERC approved a restructured
four-point design taking effect with the 2026/27 delivery year
\citep{FERC2025_ER25-1357}: an explicit price floor at
approximately \$177--\$179/MW-day eliminates the collapse to zero
price for surplus capacity. Both 2026/27 and 2027/28 cleared at
exactly the new-design Point~(a) price---which under the Shapiro/PJM
settlement embodies the agreed maximum price (the settlement cap
$\bar p_S$ described below)---motivating the analysis that follows.
Recent BRAs have run on compressed lead times relative to the
canonical three-year forward design (Table~\ref{tab:bra_timeline});
the implications are discussed in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}.

PJM's primary market-power mitigation mechanism is the Three Pivotal
Supplier (TPS) test. Supplier $i$ is pivotal in LDA $\ell$ if
removing $i$'s capacity would cause aggregate supply to fall short
of the reliability requirement. The Residual Supply Index for $k$
suppliers is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{RSI}_{k}^{\ell} \;=\;
  \frac{\text{Total capacity in LDA }\ell
        \;-\; \text{Capacity of top-}k\text{ sellers}}
       {\text{Reliability requirement for LDA }\ell}.
  \label{eq:rsi}
\end{equation}
Mitigation triggers when $\mathrm{RSI}_1 \leq 1$ or
$\mathrm{RSI}_3 \leq 1$, capping affected suppliers' offers at
their unit-specific Net Avoidable Cost Rate (Net ACR)
\citep{Bowring2013_pjm, MonitoringAnalytics2025_sotm}.
Table~\ref{tab:tps_results} summarizes RTO-level outcomes:
$\mathrm{RSI}_3 < 1$ in every year of the sample (0.62--0.73), so
the threshold is met throughout.

The high BRA clearing prices of 2025/26 and the anticipation of
similarly elevated prices in subsequent auctions triggered a
political intervention that directly modified the demand curve for
two delivery years. On December 30, 2024, the Office of Governor
Josh Shapiro filed a complaint against PJM's tariff with the
Federal Energy Regulatory Commission under Section 206 of the
Federal Power Act. The complaint argued that the VRR Point~(a)
price cap was set too high given lower expectations for new entry
in the near term, and that compressed auction lead times limited
the ability of new generation to respond to high prices in upcoming
delivery periods, so high prices would not produce efficient entry
and instead represented an unjust and unreasonable rate
\citep{PJM2025_settlement_slides}.

\label{subsec:inst_shapiro}%
Following confidential settlement discussions, PJM and the
Governor's office announced an agreement in principle on
January 28, 2025 \citep{Shapiro2025_press_release,
PJM2025_settlement_slides}. The agreement imposes a price band on
the demand curves for both 2026/27 and 2027/28 delivery years: a
price cap at approximately \$325/MW-day UCAP (denoted $\bar p_S$)
and a price floor at approximately \$175/MW-day UCAP (denoted
$\underline p_S$). The settlement does not impose an external price
constraint on an otherwise unchanged auction; instead, it modifies
the shape of the VRR demand curve itself. At prices above
$\bar p_S$ the demand curve is flat (no capacity with offers above
the cap can clear); between $\bar p_S$ and $\underline p_S$ the
existing VRR sloped segment applies; at or below $\underline p_S$
the demand curve extends horizontally to clear all available
capacity at the floor. This mechanism applies identically to the
RTO and to every LDA demand curve, subject to CETL transmission
limits \citep{PJM2025_settlement_slides, FERC2025_ER25-1357}.
Throughout the paper, $\bar p$ denotes the pre-existing
design-level Point~(a) cap ($1.5 \times \text{Net CONE}$, calibrated
to investment incentives) and $\bar p_S$ denotes the lower
settlement cap motivated by near-term consumer-cost concerns.
Table~\ref{tab:bra_timeline} shows $\bar p = \$329.17$/MW-day in
2026/27, only \$4.17/MW-day above $\bar p_S = \$325$/MW-day;
constrained-LDA Point~(a) caps were larger in prior years, so the
binding gap in those LDAs was correspondingly greater.
