\section{Institutional Background}
\label{sec:institutional}

PJM Interconnection operates the largest competitive wholesale
electricity market in North America, coordinating about 201{,}000~MW
of installed generating capacity across thirteen states and the
District of Columbia. Its capacity market, the Reliability Pricing
Model (RPM), was introduced in 2007 to procure forward commitments
to be available during a future delivery year \citep{Bowring2013_pjm,
PJM_Manual18, Pfeifenberger2011_rpm}. The market exists to address
the "missing-money" problem. Regulatory price caps in the
wholesale energy market suppress the scarcity rents that would
otherwise finance investment in peaking generation, leaving energy
revenues insufficient to recover fixed costs of new entry
\citep{Joskow2007_capacity, Joskow2008_capacity_payments,
Cramton2005_capacity}. RPM fills the gap with a forward instrument
that pays for availability and so completes the revenue stream
needed for resource adequacy.

The primary procurement mechanism is the Base Residual Auction
(BRA), a one-sided, sealed-bid, uniform-price auction held
approximately three years before the start of the delivery year it
procures. PJM acts as the administrative buyer through a publicly
announced demand schedule called the Variable Resource Requirement
(VRR) curve, and aggregates resource-level offers into an
upward-stepping supply schedule. Capacity is denominated in unforced
capacity (UCAP) terms rather than nameplate installed capacity
(ICAP). Each resource's accreditation ratio reflects the fraction
of nameplate capacity expected to deliver during system stress, with
forced outage adjustments for thermal resources and Effective Load
Carrying Capability (ELCC) adjustments for intermittent and
energy-limited resources \citep{PJM_Manual18, PJM_Manual20A}. The
reference dual fuel combustion turbine used in PJM's planning
parameters has an accreditation of approximately 79\%, so a 100~MW
nameplate unit clears as 79~MW of UCAP and is paid the clearing price
on those 79~MW. Clearing is solved jointly across the RTO and
constrained sub-Locational Devierability Areas (LDA) subject to Capacity Emergency Transfer Limit
(CETL) constraints, which bound the imports each LDA can receive
from the rest of the system. When a CETL constraint binds, the LDA's
clearing price separates from the RTO price and the constrained LDA
pays a locational premium \citep{PJM_Manual20A}.

A cleared resource receives the LDA's uniform clearing price, in
dollars per MW-day of UCAP, for every day of the
delivery year, settled monthly. A 200~MW UCAP cleared resource at a
\$300/MW-day clearing price thus receives roughly \$1.8~million per
month in capacity revenue. The cleared resource also takes on a
Capacity Performance (CP) obligation, meaning it must be available
to produce energy when PJM declares Performance Assessment Hours
during the delivery year. These are typically tight intervals when
reserves are low \citep{PJM_Manual18}. Failure to deliver during a
Performance Assessment Hour triggers a non-performance charge
calibrated to a multiple of the Net Cost of New Entry (Net CONE), and over-performance during
the same hours earns a bonus payment funded from the
non-performance charges. The CP framework is intended to align
capacity payments with delivery year availability rather than mere
ownership of nameplate capacity. The penalty mechanics themselves
are not modeled in this paper, and the SFE setup of
Section~\ref{sec:theory} treats committed capacity as delivered.

Resources whose offers do not clear in the BRA have several
alternatives. PJM conducts three Incremental Auctions (1st, 2nd,
and 3rd IA) approximately twenty, ten, and four months before the
delivery year, in which capacity needs that change after the BRA are
procured or released. These changes are driven by load forecast
revisions, planned resource cancellations, or replacement capacity
transactions. A resource that did not clear the BRA can re-offer in
any IA. Outside RPM, a load serving entity may opt out of PJM's
centralized procurement under the Fixed Resource Requirement (FRR)
Alternative, securing capacity bilaterally from generators with which
it contracts directly. FRR resources and load are removed from the
BRA and bilateral capacity arrangements substitute for centralized
clearing. A resource may also forgo capacity commitment for a
delivery year and seek revenue solely from the energy and
ancillary services markets, accepting the lost capacity payment in
exchange for operational flexibility. Finally, an existing resource
contemplating retirement may file a Notice of Deactivation. If PJM
determines that retirement would create a reliability concern that
cannot be remedied through transmission upgrades or substitute
resources, the unit may be designated Reliability Must-Run (RMR) and
continue operating under a cost-of-service agreement at a regulated
rate distinct from the BRA clearing price.

The VRR curve is the administrative demand schedule presented to
sellers in each BRA \citep{PJM_Manual18}. Under the old three-point
design (delivery years 2021/22 through 2025/26) it is parameterized
by Point~(a), the price cap at $1.5 \times \text{Net CONE}$,
Point~(b) at $0.75 \times \text{Net CONE}$ at the reliability
requirement, and Point~(c) at zero price above the reliability
requirement \citep{Bowring2013_pjm, Pfeifenberger2011_rpm}. Net CONE is the annualized cost of a
reference generating unit, net of projected energy and
ancillary-service revenues, computed independently for each LDA
\citep{PJM_Manual18, PJM_Manual20A}. FERC approved a restructured
four-point design taking effect with the 2026/27 delivery year
\citep{FERC2025_ER25-1357}. An explicit price floor at approximately
\$177--\$179/MW-day eliminates the collapse to zero price for surplus
capacity. Both 2026/27 and 2027/28 cleared at exactly the new design
Point~(a) price, which under the Shapiro/PJM settlement embodies the
agreed maximum price (the settlement cap $\bar p_S$ described below).
This pattern is what motivates the following analysis. Recent BRAs have
run on compressed lead times relative to the canonical three-year
forward design (Table~\ref{tab:bra_timeline}), and the implications
are discussed in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}.

PJM's primary market power mitigation mechanism is the Three Pivotal
Supplier (TPS) test. Supplier $i$ is pivotal in LDA $\ell$ if
removing $i$'s capacity would cause aggregate supply to fall short
of the reliability requirement. The Residual Supply Index for $k$
suppliers is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{RSI}_{k}^{\ell} \;=\;
  \frac{\text{Total capacity in LDA }\ell
        \;-\; \text{Capacity of top-}k\text{ sellers}}
       {\text{Reliability requirement for LDA }\ell}.
  \label{eq:rsi}
\end{equation}
Mitigation triggers when $\mathrm{RSI}_1 \leq 1$ or
$\mathrm{RSI}_3 \leq 1$, capping affected suppliers' offers at
their unit-specific Net Avoidable Cost Rate (Net ACR)
\citep{Bowring2013_pjm, MonitoringAnalytics2025_sotm}.
Table~\ref{tab:tps_results} summarizes RTO-level outcomes.
$\mathrm{RSI}_3 < 1$ in every year of the sample, ranging from 0.62
to 0.73, so the threshold is met throughout.

The high BRA clearing prices of 2025/26 and the anticipation of
similarly elevated prices in subsequent auctions triggered the
political intervention that directly modified the demand curve for
two delivery years. The Shapiro complaint argued that the VRR Point~(a)
price cap was set too high given lower expectations for new entry
in the near term, and that compressed auction lead times limited
the ability of new generation to respond to high prices in upcoming
delivery periods. High prices would therefore not produce efficient
entry and instead represented an unjust and unreasonable rate
\citep{PJM2025_settlement_slides}.

\label{subsec:inst_shapiro}%
The settlement agreement reached imposes a price band on
the demand curves for both 2026/27 and 2027/28 delivery years, with
a headline price cap of approximately \$325/MW-day UCAP and a price
floor of approximately \$175/MW-day UCAP. The settlement does not
impose an external price constraint on an otherwise unchanged
auction. Instead it modifies the shape of the VRR demand curve
itself. The demand curve is flat above the cap, so no capacity with
offers above the cap can clear. The existing VRR sloped segment
applies between cap and floor. At or below the floor the demand curve
extends horizontally to clear all available capacity at the floor.
This mechanism applies identically to the RTO and to every LDA demand
curve, subject to CETL transmission limits
\citep{PJM2025_settlement_slides, FERC2025_ER25-1357}.

The \$325 figure is the announced summary of the agreement
and is the number that anchors public discussion of the settlement.
FERC's implementing order \citep{FERC2025_ER25-1357} translated the
agreement into year-specific Point~(a) values of the new four-point
VRR design, setting Point~(a) at \$329.17/MW-day for 2026/27 and
\$333.44/MW-day for 2027/28 (Table~\ref{tab:bra_timeline}). These are
the operative dollar values that appear in the auction. The
gap between the headline and the FERC implementation reflects the
calibration of the new design to year-specific planning parameters.
Throughout the paper $\bar p_S$ denotes the operative new-design
Point~(a) in each capped delivery year, so $\bar p_S = \$329.17$ in
2026/27 and $\bar p_S = \$333.44$ in 2027/28. "Approximately
\$325/MW-day" is retained as narrative shorthand and is not the
dollar value used in any quantitative comparison.
