\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

On December 30, 2024, the Commonwealth of Pennsylvania filed a complaint
with the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) alleging that PJM's capacity
auction demand curve was miscalibrated, with its price cap set too high given
near term expectations for new entry. The complaint cited PJM's July 2024
Base Residual Auction for the 2025/26 delivery year, which cleared at
\$269.92/MW-day, an increase of more than 800 percent from the prior year,
as evidence that the existing market design imposed unnecessary costs on
65 million consumers across 13 states \citep{Shapiro2025_press_release}.
Six weeks later, on January 28, 2025, Governor Josh Shapiro announced an
agreement with PJM in which the demand curves for the 2026/27 and 2027/28
auctions would be modified to impose a price cap of approximately
\$325/MW-day UCAP and a price floor of approximately
\$175/MW-day UCAP, applied to both the RTO and all constrained Locational
Deliverability Areas \citep{PJM2025_settlement_slides}. The agreement was
projected to save consumers over \$21 billion over the two delivery years.

The settlement reshapes PJM's VRR demand
curve by imposing a cap at $\bar{p}_S \approx \$325$/MW-day below the
existing design-level cap at Point~(a), and adding a floor at
$\bar{p}_f \approx \$175$/MW-day (Section~\ref{subsec:inst_shapiro}).
This paper asks what price PJM's 2026/27 and 2027/28 auctions would have
cleared at absent the settlement modification, and what the consequences of
suppressing prices to $\bar{p}_S$ are.

I answer the question using a calibrated SFE
model of the PJM Base Residual Auction. In the model, $K$ symmetric
strategic sellers simultaneously submit supply functions against PJM's VRR
demand curve, which is publicly known before bidding. Following
\citet{Klemperer1989_sfe} and \citet{Green1992_british}, equilibrium is
characterized by a first order ordinary differential equation (ODE) system
in the sellers' supply functions, integrated backward from the
capacity constrained boundary. Equilibrium selection imposes the Holmberg
boundary condition, requiring each seller's supply function to equal installed
capacity at the VRR Point~(a) cap. This criterion is motivated by
\citet{Holmberg2008_unique_sfe}'s uniqueness result for stochastic markets;
in the deterministic BRA it is imposed as a selection device. Because the
boundary condition forces $p^* = \bar{p}$ whenever it is active, the 2026/27
and 2027/28 at-cap outcomes are consistent with the maintained assumption of
$K \leq 3$ strategic sellers but do not independently test the dollar value
of the clearing price; the model's substantive content is the
\emph{regime}---at-cap versus interior---and its dependence on $K$, together
with the markup level when the equilibrium is interior. I calibrate the
model to all seven completed BRA delivery years (2021/22 through 2027/28)
using published PJM planning parameters, BRA clearing results, and
Independent Market Monitor (IMM) State of the Market cost data. The SFE
equilibrium price $p^*$ is the counterfactual clearing price under strategic
bidding against the unmodified VRR curve; when the boundary condition is
active, $p^*$ equals the design-level Point~(a) cap by construction, which
is what distinguishes the SFE counterfactual from the VRR Point~(a)
benchmark used in the settlement's \$21~billion savings figure.
