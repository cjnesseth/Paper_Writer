\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

On December 30, 2024, the Commonwealth of Pennsylvania filed a complaint
with the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission alleging that PJM's capacity
auction demand curve was miscalibrated, with its price cap set too high given
near-term expectations for new entry. The complaint cited PJM's July 2024
Base Residual Auction for the 2025/26 delivery year, which cleared at
\$269.92/MW-day, an increase of more than 800 percent from the prior year,
as evidence that the existing market design imposed unnecessary costs on
65 million consumers across 13 states \citep{Shapiro2025_press_release}.
Six weeks later, on January 28, 2025, Governor Josh Shapiro announced an
agreement with PJM in which the demand curves for the 2026/27 and 2027/28
auctions would be modified to impose a price cap of approximately
\$325/MW-day (unforced capacity, UCAP) and a price floor of approximately
\$175/MW-day UCAP, applied to both the RTO and all constrained Locational
Deliverability Areas \citep{PJM2025_settlement_slides}. The agreement was
projected to save consumers over \$21 billion over the two delivery years.

The settlement creates a two-tier price ceiling in PJM's capacity market.
PJM's existing Variable Resource Requirement (VRR) curve already contains a
design-level cap at Point~(a), set at approximately 1.5 times the Net Cost
of New Entry (CONE), serving as an investment-adequacy ceiling calibrated to
the annualized cost of a reference generating unit and intended to prevent
prices from permanently exceeding the entry threshold. The settlement imposes
a second, lower cap at $\bar{p}_S \approx \$325$/MW-day, below the Point~(a)
level, while simultaneously adding a floor at $\bar{p}_f \approx \$175$/MW-day
that clears all capacity offered at or below that level. The mechanism is
implemented by reshaping the demand curve itself, giving it a flat top at
$\bar{p}_S$, the existing sloped segment between $\bar{p}_S$ and
$\bar{p}_f$, and a flat bottom at $\bar{p}_f$ \citep{PJM2025_settlement_slides}.
This paper asks what price PJM's 2026/27 and 2027/28 auctions would have
cleared at absent the settlement modification, and what the consequences of
suppressing prices to $\bar{p}_S$ are.

I answer the question using a calibrated Supply Function Equilibrium (SFE)
model of the PJM Base Residual Auction. In the model, $K$ symmetric
strategic sellers simultaneously submit supply functions against PJM's VRR
demand curve, which is publicly known before bidding. Following
\citet{Klemperer1989_sfe} and \citet{Green1992_british}, equilibrium is
characterized by a first-order ordinary differential equation (ODE) system
in the sellers' supply functions, integrated backward from the
capacity-constrained boundary. Equilibrium selection imposes the Holmberg
boundary condition, requiring each seller's supply function to equal installed
capacity at the VRR Point~(a) cap. This criterion is motivated by
\citet{Holmberg2008_unique_sfe}'s uniqueness result for stochastic markets;
in the deterministic BRA it is imposed as a selection device, and the at-cap
clearing outcomes in 2026/27 and 2027/28 confirm the condition is active in
practice. I calibrate the model to three benchmark delivery years using
published PJM planning parameters, BRA clearing results, and IMM State of
the Market cost data. The SFE equilibrium price $p^*$ is the counterfactual
price at which the unmodified VRR curve would have cleared under strategic
bidding, which is what is needed to evaluate the settlement's claim of
\$21 billion in savings.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:literature} surveys the supply function equilibrium
literature, the capacity market design literature, and the economics of
price controls in oligopolistic markets. Section~\ref{sec:institutional}
reviews PJM's institutional context, including the VRR mechanism, auction
irregularities, and the Shapiro settlement. Section~\ref{sec:model}
develops the theoretical SFE model. Section~\ref{sec:calibration} describes
data sources and calibration. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents SFE
equilibrium prices and comparative statics relative to the settlement cap.
Section~\ref{sec:allocative_cost} analyzes the allocative consequences of
the settlement's price band for entry, exit, and information.
Section~\ref{sec:leadtime} evaluates the lead-time argument underlying
the complaint. Section~\ref{sec:21billion} examines the \$21~billion
savings claim. Section~\ref{sec:policy_alternatives} considers structural
alternatives to price caps. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.
