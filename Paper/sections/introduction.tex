\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

In June 2025 and January 2026, PJM Interconnection cleared its Base Residual
Auctions for the 2026/27 and 2027/28 delivery years at prices of
\$329.17/MW-day and \$333.44/MW-day respectively --- both equal to the
maximum price permitted by PJM's Variable Resource Requirement curve. These
outcomes, roughly ten times the prices prevailing in 2023 and 2024, coincide
with a rapid acceleration in data-center load growth across the PJM footprint
and a structural reform to the VRR mechanism mandated by FERC Order
ER25-1357. Whether these clearing prices reflect genuine scarcity or whether
strategic suppliers extracted rents from a demand curve whose shape they
anticipated is a question central to the design and regulation of capacity
markets \citep{Joskow2007_capacity}. This paper answers that question
structurally, using a calibrated supply function equilibrium model.

The existing tool for detecting market power in PJM's capacity auctions is
the Three Pivotal Supplier (TPS) test administered by the Independent Market
Monitor \citep{Bowring2013_pjm}. A seller passes the test if the residual
supply index --- the ratio of available supply excluding that seller to the
reliability requirement --- exceeds unity; failing sellers face offer price
mitigation. In our data, the RTO-level RSI for the top three sellers ranges
from 0.62 to 0.73 across all seven delivery years in the sample, meaning
that every participant fails the TPS test in every year. Yet over this same
period, the clearing price varies by a factor of eleven, from \$28.92/MW-day
in 2024/25 to \$333.44/MW-day in 2027/28. The binary pass/fail structure of
the TPS test is blind to this variation: it can detect the presence of market
power, but it cannot quantify its magnitude, characterize how markups change
with market structure, or evaluate how VRR redesign affects equilibrium
prices. A richer structural measure is needed.

We develop a calibrated supply function equilibrium model of the PJM Base
Residual Auction. In the model, $K$ symmetric strategic sellers simultaneously
submit supply functions before the auction clears. The auction demand is
PJM's piecewise-linear VRR curve, which is publicly known before bidding.
Following \citet{Klemperer1989_sfe} and \citet{Green1992_british}, equilibrium
is characterized by a first-order ordinary differential equation system in
the sellers' supply functions, integrated backward from the capacity-
constrained boundary. The equilibrium is unique because the binding-capacity
conditions of \citet{Holmberg2008_unique_sfe} are satisfied: PJM's
reliability requirement implies positive probability that aggregate supply
reaches the capacity ceiling at the VRR Point~(a) price cap, tying the
arbitrary constant in the ODE and selecting a unique supply function
trajectory. We calibrate the model to three benchmark delivery years ---
2023/24 (low price, old VRR), 2025/26 (price spike, old VRR), and 2026/27
(at-cap, new VRR) --- using published PJM planning parameters, BRA clearing
results, and IMM State of the Market cost data.

Four findings emerge. First, the baseline Lerner indices under $K = 3$
symmetric strategic sellers are 0.54--0.65 across the three benchmark years.
The 2026/27 equilibrium clears exactly at the VRR price cap (\$329.17/MW-day),
matching the observed outcome without any calibration to the actual price ---
a direct validation of the Holmberg boundary condition. Second, concentration
matters sharply: at $K = 4$, Lerner indices fall by 33--45 percentage points
relative to $K = 3$; at $K = 7$, the equilibrium is nearly competitive.
The observed 2026/27 at-cap outcome is consistent with $K \leq 3$ and
inconsistent with $K \geq 4$, providing identification of the effective number
of strategic sellers. Third, the VRR redesign (FERC ER25-1357) steepens
the sloped segment between the floor and cap, which reduces markups in the
interior, but the new price floor at $p_f \approx \$177$/MW-day prevents
prices from falling below that level even in competitive scenarios. Fourth,
import capacity expansions --- modeled as increases in the competitive fringe
available to the RTO --- reduce equilibrium prices substantially: a 30\%
fringe expansion cuts the Lerner index by 12--18 percentage points in
interior-clearing years, with larger effects in the at-cap 2026/27 year.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:literature} surveys the supply function equilibrium
literature and the capacity market design literature.
