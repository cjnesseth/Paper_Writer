\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:literature}

The supply function equilibrium concept, formalized for the demand-uncertainty
case by \citet{Klemperer1989_sfe}, considers an oligopoly in which each firm
commits to a schedule mapping prices to quantities before demand is realized.
Unlike Bertrand or Cournot competition, the supply function strategy allows
each firm to adapt its output to whatever market price emerges after the demand
shock is realized. \citeauthor{Klemperer1989_sfe} show that symmetric Nash
equilibria are characterized by a first-order differential equation whose
solutions form a continuum bounded above by the Cournot schedule and below by
the Bertrand schedule (which coincides with the competitive schedule $p = c$
under constant marginal cost, as in the present calibration), with demand
uncertainty disciplining firms to choose upward-sloping supply functions.

\citet{Green1992_british} apply the Klemperer-Meyer framework to the England
and Wales electricity market, establishing the methodological template I follow: derive the symmetric ODE, integrate backward from the capacity-constrained
boundary, and calibrate to observed market structure.
\citet{Holmberg2008_unique_sfe} shows that in capacity constrained electricity
markets, the symmetric SFE is unique when rivals'
capacity constraints simultaneously bind with positive probability, tying the
ODE boundary condition and selecting a unique equilibrium trajectory. This paper applies that boundary condition as an equilibrium selection device in the deterministic BRA. For the asymmetric
extension, \citet{Anderson2008_sfe_asymmetric} develop a discretization-based
approximation scheme with provable convergence as the demand-shock grid is
refined. \citet{Holmberg2007_asymmetric} establishes existence and uniqueness
results for the constant marginal cost, asymmetric capacity case.
\citet{Rudkevich1998_poolco} provide an early application of supply function
analysis to competitive poolco electricity markets, bridging the canonical
Klemperer-Meyer theory and applied generation market modeling. The symmetric
case applies here and I solve the ODE backward from the Holmberg boundary
using the deSolve package in R.

The capacity market design literature originates from the "missing money"
problem, where competitive energy markets, subject to regulatory price caps, suppress
the scarcity rents that would otherwise finance investment in peaking generation
\citep{Joskow2007_capacity, Joskow2008_capacity_payments}. PJM's Variable
Resource Requirement curve is a downward-sloping administrative demand schedule
that adds elasticity relative to the vertical demand curve it replaced and
integrates scarcity pricing into the capacity clearing process
\citep{Bowring2013_pjm}. This paper takes the
VRR design as given and analyzes how its slope governs strategic markup
opportunities under concentrated supply.

\citet{Weitzman1974_prices} shows that the choice between price and quantity
regulatory instruments depends on the relative slopes of marginal benefit and
marginal cost. When supply is inelastic in the short run, price instruments
dominate. \citet{GlaeserLuttmer2003_rent} demonstrate in the rent control context that
binding price controls generate consumption misallocation qualitatively
different from the standard deadweight triangle. The
analysis in Section~\ref{sec:allocative_cost} draws on these results to
characterize the allocative consequences of the settlement's price band.
\citet{Cramton2005_capacity} emphasize that a central function of the capacity
market is to provide a long-run investment signal. A binding cap disrupts
this signal precisely when it is most informative.
