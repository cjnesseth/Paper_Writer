% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Supply Function Equilibria: Theory}
\label{subsec:lit_sfe_theory}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The supply function equilibrium concept, introduced by
\citet{Klemperer1989_sfe}, considers an oligopoly in which each firm commits
to a schedule mapping prices to quantities before demand is realized. Unlike
Bertrand or Cournot competition, the supply function strategy allows each
firm to adapt its output to whatever market price emerges after the demand
shock is realized. \citeauthor{Klemperer1989_sfe} show that symmetric Nash
equilibria are characterized by a first-order differential equation whose
solutions form a continuum between the competitive schedule and the Cournot
schedule, with demand uncertainty disciplining firms to choose upward-sloping
supply functions.

\citet{Green1992_british} apply the Klemperer-Meyer framework to the England
and Wales electricity market, establishing the methodological template followed
here: derive the symmetric ODE, integrate backward from the capacity-constrained
boundary, and calibrate to observed market structure.
\citet{Holmberg2008_unique_sfe} shows that in capacity-constrained electricity
markets, the supply function equilibrium is unique when rivals' capacity
constraints simultaneously bind, tying the ODE boundary condition and selecting
a unique equilibrium trajectory. This paper applies that boundary condition as
an equilibrium selection device in the deterministic BRA. For the asymmetric
extension, \citet{Anderson2008_sfe_asymmetric} provide a convergent iterative
algorithm. The symmetric case applies here and I solve the ODE backward from
the Holmberg boundary using the \texttt{deSolve} package in R.

\subsection{Capacity Market Design}
\label{subsec:lit_capacity_markets}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The capacity market design literature originates from the ``missing money''
problem: competitive energy markets, subject to regulatory price caps, suppress
the scarcity rents that would otherwise finance investment in peaking generation
\citep{Joskow2007_capacity, Joskow2008_capacity_payments}. PJM's Variable
Resource Requirement curve is a downward-sloping administrative demand schedule
designed to moderate the extreme price volatility that vertical demand generates
near the reliability threshold \citep{Bowring2013_pjm}. This paper takes the
VRR design as given and analyzes how its slope governs strategic markup
opportunities under concentrated supply.

\subsection{Price Controls in Oligopolistic Markets}
\label{subsec:lit_price_controls}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\citet{Weitzman1974_prices} shows that the choice between price and quantity
regulatory instruments depends on the relative slopes of marginal benefit and
marginal cost. When supply is inelastic in the short run, price instruments
dominate. \citet{GlaeserLuttmer2003_rent} demonstrate that binding price
controls in imperfectly competitive markets generate misallocation qualitatively
different from the standard deadweight triangle. In the SFE literature, the
analysis in Section~\ref{sec:allocative_cost} draws on these results to
characterize the allocative consequences of the settlement's price band.
\citet{Cramton2005_capacity} emphasize that the capacity market's primary
function is to provide a long-run investment signal. A binding cap disrupts
this signal precisely when it is most informative.
