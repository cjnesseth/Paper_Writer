\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:literature}

The supply function equilibrium concept, formalized by
\citet{Klemperer1989_sfe} for demand-uncertainty oligopolies in which
each firm commits to a price-quantity schedule before demand realizes,
yields symmetric Nash equilibria characterized by a first-order
differential equation whose solutions form a continuum bounded above by
the Cournot schedule and below by the competitive schedule $p = c$.
\citet{Green1992_british} apply this framework to the England and Wales
electricity market, establishing the methodological template I follow:
derive the symmetric ODE, integrate backward from the capacity-constrained
boundary, and calibrate to observed market structure.
\citet{Holmberg2008_unique_sfe} shows that in capacity-constrained
electricity markets the symmetric SFE is unique when rivals' capacity
constraints simultaneously bind with positive probability, providing the
boundary condition I use as an equilibrium selection device in the
deterministic BRA. For the asymmetric extension,
\citet{Anderson2008_sfe_asymmetric} and \citet{Holmberg2007_asymmetric}
develop existence and convergence results;
\citet{Rudkevich1998_poolco} bridges the canonical Klemperer-Meyer
theory and applied generation-market modeling. I solve the symmetric
ODE backward from the Holmberg boundary using the deSolve package in R.

The capacity market design literature originates from the "missing money"
problem, where competitive energy markets, subject to regulatory price caps, suppress
the scarcity rents that would otherwise finance investment in peaking generation
\citep{Joskow2007_capacity, Joskow2008_capacity_payments}. PJM's Variable
Resource Requirement curve is a downward-sloping administrative demand schedule
that adds elasticity relative to the vertical demand curve it replaced and
integrates scarcity pricing into the capacity clearing process
\citep{Bowring2013_pjm}. This paper takes the
VRR design as given and analyzes how its slope governs strategic markup
opportunities under concentrated supply.

\citet{Weitzman1974_prices} shows that the choice between price and quantity
regulatory instruments depends on the relative slopes of marginal benefit and
marginal cost. When supply is inelastic in the short run, price instruments
dominate. \citet{GlaeserLuttmer2003_rent} demonstrate in the rent control context that
binding price controls generate consumption misallocation qualitatively
different from the standard deadweight triangle.
\citet{Cramton2005_capacity} emphasize that a central function of the capacity
market is to provide a long-run investment signal: a binding cap disrupts
this signal precisely when it is most informative, motivating the
allocative discussion in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}.
