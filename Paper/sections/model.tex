\section{Framework and Calibration}
\label{sec:theory}
\label{subsec:theory_ceiling}%

Standard price control theory's prediction is that when the regulator imposes a
cap $\bar p_S$ below the unconstrained clearing price $p^*$, demand at
$\bar p_S$ exceeds the quantity sellers will provide. The gap between
$D(\bar p_S)$ and $S(\bar p_S)$ is the shortage.
\citet{Weitzman1974_prices} formalizes the choice between price and
quantity instruments under uncertainty, and
\citet{GlaeserLuttmer2003_rent} document a separate allocative
consequence of binding ceilings. Binding ceilings misallocate
consumption across heterogeneous demanders, beyond the standard
deadweight triangle.

The Shapiro settlement applies this framework directly to PJM's Reliability
Pricing Model. The settlement modifies the VRR
curve to be flat for all $p \geq \bar p_S$ and adds a flat segment at
$\underline p_S$ for low prices, with $\bar p_S = \$329.17$/MW-day UCAP
in 2026/27 and $\bar p_S = \$333.44$/MW-day in 2027/28 and a corresponding
floor of approximately \$177--\$180/MW-day
(Section~\ref{subsec:inst_shapiro}). The capped auction therefore clears
whatever capacity sellers offer at or below $\bar p_S$. If aggregate
offers at the cap fall short of the reliability requirement $q_b$ (the
Point~(b) quantity in the VRR curve), the auction clears at
$(\bar p_S, Q_{\text{offered}})$ with $Q_{\text{offered}} < q_b$, and
two consequences are visible separately. The price signal is cut off
on the upper segment, and where supply is tight the cleared quantity
falls short of the planning target. Which of these channels was
operative in 2026/27 and 2027/28 is the empirical question I aim to answer.

\label{subsec:theory_sfe}%
To distinguish a binding cap from a redundant one, this paper uses the
symmetric supply function equilibrium (SFE) of
\citet{Klemperer1989_sfe} as a characterization of the unconstrained
equilibrium price. The setup follows the calibrated-electricity-market
template of \citet{Green1992_british}. There are $K$ symmetric pivotal
sellers with constant marginal cost $c$ submitting nondecreasing supply
schedules $S_i(\cdot)$ against the publicly known VRR demand curve
$D(\cdot)$, alongside a competitive fringe of size $\bar Q_f$ pricing
at $c$. Equilibrium is selected by the
\citet{Holmberg2008_unique_sfe} boundary condition, which requires each
seller's supply function to attain its capacity at the operative
Point~(a) cap, denoted $\bar p$. In capped delivery years $\bar p$
coincides with the settlement value $\bar p_S$ described
in Section~\ref{subsec:inst_shapiro}. In pre-settlement years $\bar p$
is the old-design Point~(a) ($1.5 \times \text{Net CONE}$). Under
symmetry the equilibrium reduces to a scalar first-order ODE in the
per-firm supply function $s(p)$, integrated backward from
$s(\bar p) = \bar q$. The derivation is standard
\citep{Klemperer1989_sfe, Green1992_british, Rudkevich1998_poolco} and
is not reproduced here.

The model's content for this paper is binary. The at-cap regime
($p^* = \bar p$) applies whenever rivals' capacity constraints
simultaneously bind, that is, when $K$ pivotal sellers cannot meet
residual demand at any interior price. Otherwise the interior regime
($p^* < \bar p$) holds. Solving the symmetric
ODE for the calibrations of Section~\ref{sec:calibration} gives the
at-cap regime for $K \leq 3$ and the interior regime for $K \geq 4$,
with the price-cost margin falling by roughly 20 to 25 percentage points
across this regime change.

PJM's auction concentration fits the $K \leq 3$ regime. The IMM reports
$\mathrm{RSI}_3 < 1$ in every sample year
(Table~\ref{tab:tps_results}), meaning the three largest sellers are
collectively pivotal at the reliability requirement. A symmetric $K=3$
calibration is therefore the natural choice and is maintained
throughout.

The SFE plays a narrow role in my analysis, functioning as a way to show
that, under the IMM-reported concentration, the relevant unconstrained
equilibrium belongs to the at-cap regime. In that regime the SFE
matches the observed clearing price by construction, not as an
independent empirical test, and the dollar value of $\bar p$ is read
directly from PJM's planning parameters. The classification rests on
a symmetric representation of the three pivotal sellers, and a fully
asymmetric calibration could shift the regime boundary. The IMM-reported pivotality and the persistent gap between sector average avoidable cost rate (ACR)
and observed clearing prices both point in the at-cap direction, but
the symmetry assumption is the most prominent caveat on the regime
claim.
