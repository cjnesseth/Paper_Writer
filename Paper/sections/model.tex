\section{Model}
\label{sec:model}

\label{subsec:model_setup}

The BRA is modeled as a static game in which sellers simultaneously submit
supply functions before the auction clears. This formulation follows
\citet{Klemperer1989_sfe}, whose original framework admits demand
uncertainty, with \citet{Green1992_british} applying it to electricity
markets under deterministic demand. The VRR curve is deterministic, so
I use the Klemperer-Meyer ODE as an equilibrium selection device rather
than as the equilibrium concept itself. Timing: (1)~PJM publishes the VRR
parameters for the upcoming delivery year; (2)~sellers submit supply
functions; (3)~PJM clears the auction at the uniform price where aggregate
supply meets the VRR curve; (4)~sellers deliver committed capacity during
the delivery year.

\begin{assumption}
  \label{assm:players}
  There are $K \geq 2$ strategic sellers indexed $i = 1, \ldots, K$ and a
  competitive fringe. Each seller $i$ holds a fixed capacity
  portfolio $\bar{q}_i > 0$ measured in megawatts. A supply function
  $S_i: [0, \bar{p}] \to [0, \bar{q}_i]$ is a nondecreasing, twice
  continuously differentiable mapping from price to quantity. Each seller
  simultaneously commits to a supply function before the auction clears.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}
  \label{assm:fringe}
  The competitive fringe bids at its avoidable cost rate $c_f > 0$. Fringe
  supply is $S_f(p) = 0$ for $p < c_f$ and $S_f(p) = \bar{Q}_f$ for
  $p \geq c_f$, where $\bar{Q}_f > 0$ is total fringe capacity. The fringe
  is price taking and its behavior is common knowledge.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}
  \label{assm:cost}
  Each strategic seller has a constant marginal cost $c > 0$ for output
  $q \leq \bar{q}_i$ and infinite marginal cost above capacity. For the
  symmetric calibration I set $c$ equal to the sector average avoidable
  cost rate published by the PJM IMM.
\end{assumption}

The market clears at the price $p^*$ satisfying
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{i=1}^{K} S_i(p^*) + S_f(p^*) = D(p^*),
  \label{eq:clearing}
\end{equation}
where $D(\cdot)$ is the VRR demand schedule described below. Every seller
receives the same clearing price $p^*$ for its committed capacity. The
residual demand facing seller $i$ at price $p$ is
\begin{equation}
  R_i(p) \;=\; D(p) - \sum_{j \neq i} S_j(p) - S_f(p).
  \label{eq:resid}
\end{equation}
In equilibrium, $S_i(p^*) = R_i(p^*)$. Because $D(\cdot)$ and all rivals'
strategies are publicly known before the auction, each seller's residual
demand is deterministic. The boundary condition below selects among
equilibria.

\label{subsec:model_vrr}

PJM's publicly announced VRR curve is a piecewise linear, downward sloping demand schedule. Under the old design (delivery years 2021/22 to 2025/26) it is parameterized by three anchor points
$(p_a, q_a)$, $(p_b, q_b)$, and $(0, q_c)$:
\begin{equation}
  D(p) =
  \begin{cases}
    q_a                                              & p \geq p_a \\[4pt]
    q_a + \dfrac{q_b - q_a}{p_b - p_a}(p - p_a)   & p_b \leq p < p_a \\[6pt]
    q_b + \dfrac{q_c - q_b}{0 - p_b}(p - p_b)     & 0 \leq p < p_b,
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:vrr_old}
\end{equation}
where $p_a = 1.5 \times \text{Net CONE}$ is the price cap and
$p_b = 0.75 \times \text{Net CONE}$ is the reliability requirement price.
The quantities $q_a < q_b < q_c$ are the corresponding capacity levels, with
$q_b$ equal to the reliability requirement. Because $D(p) = q_a$ for all
$p \geq p_a$, the VRR curve is effectively inelastic above the cap.

Under the new design (delivery years 2026/27 onward), FERC Order ER25-1357
introduces a fourth anchor point $(p_f, q_f)$ and $(p_f, q_d)$ with
$q_f > q_b$, creating a flat floor segment at price $p_f \approx \$177$--$\$179$/MW-day.
 The new schedule is:
\begin{equation}
  D(p) =
  \begin{cases}
    q_a                                              & p \geq p_a \\[4pt]
    q_a + \dfrac{q_b - q_a}{p_f - p_a}(p - p_a)   & p_f \leq p < p_a \\[6pt]
    q_d                                              & 0 \leq p < p_f.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:vrr_new}
\end{equation}
The flat floor segment at $p_f$ means that clearing prices cannot fall below
$p_f$ along the VRR, changing the elasticity profile that sellers face and
thus the equilibrium markup. The comparative static in
Section~\ref{subsec:res_vrr} isolates this design change.
The slope $D'(p)$ is piecewise constant and satisfies

\begin{assumption}
  \label{assm:demand}
  $D'(p) < 0$ on the sloped segments; $D'(p) = 0$ on the flat segments
  (at and above $p_a$, and below $p_f$ under the new design). The function
  $D(\cdot)$ is known to all players before the auction.
\end{assumption}

\label{subsec:model_foc}

Seller $i$ chooses $S_i(\cdot)$ to maximize profit. Taking rivals'
supply functions as fixed, the profit at any market clearing price $p$ is
$\pi_i = p \cdot S_i(p) - c \cdot S_i(p) = (p - c) \cdot S_i(p)$, since
marginal cost is constant at $c$. The seller's problem is equivalent to
maximizing profit along its residual demand curve $R_i(\cdot)$. At
price $p$, if seller $i$ considers increasing its offered quantity by
$\mathrm{d}q$, the market clearing price must fall by $\mathrm{d}p$ to
preserve equation~\eqref{eq:clearing}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{d}p
  \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{d}q}{D'(p) - \sum_{j \neq i} S_j'(p) - S_f'(p)}.
  \label{eq:dprice}
\end{equation}
Since $D'(p) < 0$ and $S_j'(p) \geq 0$, the denominator is strictly
negative, so any increase in supply lowers the clearing price. The seller's
marginal revenue from increasing quantity at price $p$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \text{MR}_i(p)
  \;=\; p + \frac{S_i(p)}{D'(p) - \sum_{j \neq i} S_j'(p) - S_f'(p)}.
  \label{eq:mr}
\end{equation}
Setting marginal revenue equal to marginal cost $c$ yields the
\emph{supply function equilibrium condition}:

\begin{proposition}
  \label{prop:sfe_foc}
  In a supply function equilibrium, each seller $i$'s strategy
  $S_i(\cdot)$ satisfies
  \begin{equation}
    S_i(p) \;=\; -\bigl(p - c\bigr)
    \Bigl[D'(p) - \textstyle\sum_{j \neq i} S_j'(p) - S_f'(p)\Bigr]
    \label{eq:sfe_foc}
  \end{equation}
  at every price $p$ at which the market can clear. This is a system of
  $K$ coupled first-order ordinary differential equations in the supply
  functions $\{S_1, \ldots, S_K\}$.
\end{proposition}

Equation~\eqref{eq:sfe_foc} is the $K$-seller generalization of the
first-order condition derived by \citet{Green1992_british} for the symmetric
duopoly (their equation~3) and of the general condition in
\citet{Anderson2008_sfe_asymmetric} (their equation~1). Its economic content
is straightforward: seller $i$ equates its residual-demand markup $(p - c)$
to the inverse of the residual demand elasticity at that price. When more
sellers compete ($K$ large) or the VRR slope is steep (large $|D'|$), the
system requires a smaller markup at each price, pushing the equilibrium
supply function toward the competitive schedule.

\label{subsec:model_selection}

The ODE system~\eqref{eq:sfe_foc} generally admits a continuum of
solutions. \citet{Klemperer1989_sfe} show that under demand uncertainty
with bounded support, the symmetric SFE trajectories form a connected
interval bounded above by the Cournot schedule and below by the Bertrand
schedule (which coincides with $p = c$ under constant marginal cost). Any
trajectory between these stationary solutions is a Nash equilibrium, so
prediction and comparative statics require a selection criterion. I use
the following boundary condition, which applies in the capacity auction
setting:

\begin{proposition}[\citealt{Holmberg2008_unique_sfe}]
  \label{prop:holmberg}
  Suppose (i) sellers are symmetric, (ii) demand is perfectly inelastic below
  the price cap $\bar{p}$ and zero above, (iii) seller cost is strictly
  convex, twice continuously differentiable, with marginal cost below the cap
  at full capacity, and (iv) the probability that aggregate demand reaches
  the capacity ceiling is strictly positive. Then there exists a unique
  symmetric supply function equilibrium. In this equilibrium each seller's
  supply function satisfies the boundary condition
  \begin{equation}
    S_i(\bar{p}) \;=\; \bar{q}_i \quad \text{for all } i.
    \label{eq:bc}
  \end{equation}
\end{proposition}

Holmberg's original proof is under demand uncertainty. In the deterministic
BRA studied here I impose~\eqref{eq:bc} as an equilibrium selection device
rather than deriving it from the theorem.

The intuition: when rivals' capacity constraints simultaneously bind,
seller $i$ faces perfectly inelastic residual demand and its optimum is
to price at the cap $\bar{p}$. Any lower price sacrifices revenue without
gaining quantity, and any higher price is infeasible. This forces the
supply function through $(\bar{p},\, \bar{q}_i)$, pinning the arbitrary
constant in the ODE. In the PJM BRA, $\bar{p}$ equals the VRR Point~(a)
price, and Assumption~\ref{assm:demand} gives $D'(\bar{p}) = 0$ (the VRR
is flat at the cap). The at-cap clearing outcomes in 2026/27 and 2027/28
(Table~\ref{tab:bra_timeline}) confirm the boundary condition is active
in those years, consistent with the \citet{Holmberg2008_unique_sfe}
selection criterion. PJM's reliability target requires procuring nearly
all installed capacity, so binding-capacity outcomes are the empirically
relevant case. I therefore integrate~\eqref{eq:sfe_foc} \emph{backward}
from the boundary condition $S_i(\bar{p}) = \bar{q}_i$.

\label{subsec:model_symmetric}

For calibration and comparative statics, I begin with the symmetric case in
which all $K$ strategic sellers are identical with $\bar{q}_i = \bar{q}$ and
$c_i = c$ for all $i$. In a symmetric Nash equilibrium,
$S_i(p) = s(p)$ for all $i$. Substituting into
equation~\eqref{eq:sfe_foc}:
\begin{equation}
  s(p) \;=\; -\bigl(p - c\bigr)
    \Bigl[D'(p) - (K-1)\,s'(p) - S_f'(p)\Bigr].
  \label{eq:sfe_sym_raw}
\end{equation}
Under Assumption~\ref{assm:fringe}, $S_f'(p) = 0$ almost everywhere.
Rearranging~\eqref{eq:sfe_sym_raw} for $s'(p)$ yields the symmetric
SFE ordinary differential equation
\begin{equation}
    s'(p) \;=\; \frac{1}{K-1}
    \left[ D'(p) + \frac{s(p)}{p - c} \right],
    \quad p \in [c,\, \bar{p}),
  \label{eq:sfe_ode}
\end{equation}
with boundary condition $s(\bar{p}) = \bar{q}$. This is a first-order,
nonautonomous ODE in the single unknown function $s(\cdot)$. It is
the $K$-firm generalization of \citeauthor{Green1992_british}'s
equation~(3), to which it reduces when $K = 2$ and $S_f \equiv 0$.

Equation~\eqref{eq:sfe_ode} has a direct economic interpretation. The
$D'(p)$ term captures the direct effect of price on demand: when demand is
more responsive (larger $|D'|$), the equilibrium supply function is
steeper in price. The $s(p)/(p-c)$ term is a markup-boundary feedback:
as $p \to c$ from above, it diverges and keeps $s'(p)$ bounded, enforcing
the lower boundary and ruling out below-cost offers. Well above $c$, the
feedback partially offsets $D'(p)$, so the supply function is steeper
than a linear interpolation between $c$ and $\bar{p}$ would predict.
More competitors (larger $K$) divide the denominator and flatten each
firm's supply function toward the competitive benchmark.

The equilibrium clearing price $p^*$ is found by solving
\begin{equation}
  K \cdot s(p^*) + S_f(p^*) = D(p^*)
  \label{eq:clearing_sym}
\end{equation}
for $p^*$ after integrating~\eqref{eq:sfe_ode} backward from $\bar{p}$.
The implied price-cost margin (Lerner index) is
\begin{equation}
  L \;=\; \frac{p^* - c}{p^*}
  \label{eq:lerner}
\end{equation}
which measures the equilibrium price-cost margin reported in
Section~\ref{sec:results}. The competitive benchmark corresponds to $c = p^*$
($L = 0$), attained as $K$ approaches infinity or as $|D'(p)|$ approaches infinity (perfectly
elastic demand). The Cournot upper bound on the markup is
$L^{\text{Cournot}} = 1 / (K \cdot |D'(p^*) \cdot p^* / D(p^*)|)$, the
standard inverse elasticity rule evaluated at market demand (which equals the
residual demand elasticity in the symmetric case).

Treating the three largest PJM sellers as symmetric is a tractability
assumption. It reduces the $K$-dimensional coupled ODE
system~\eqref{eq:sfe_foc} to the scalar ODE~\eqref{eq:sfe_ode}, which is
calibratable from publicly available aggregate data. This is the standard
approach in the calibrated SFE literature \citep{Green1992_british,
Rudkevich1998_poolco}. The symmetric Lerner index approximates the
capacity-weighted average markup; asymmetric extensions are left for
future work. Equation~\eqref{eq:sfe_ode} is solved numerically with a
fourth-order Runge-Kutta integrator using fixed step size $h =
0.05$\,\$/MW-day, integrating backward from $s(\bar{p}) = \bar{q}$.
