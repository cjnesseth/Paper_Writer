\section{Results: Equilibrium Prices vs.\ the Shapiro Settlement}
\label{sec:results}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Baseline Equilibrium}
\label{subsec:res_baseline}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Table~\ref{tab:baseline} presents the baseline symmetric SFE results for the
three benchmark delivery years, calibrated with $K = 3$ strategic sellers and
$c = \$150$/MW-day (the sector-average ACR for combined-cycle generators from
the 2025 IMM State of the Market). For each year, the table reports the
model-predicted equilibrium price $p^*$, the observed BRA clearing price, and
the implied price-cost margin (Lerner index) $L = (p^* - c)/p^*$.

    \hline

    2025/26 & Old & \$452 & ---        & \$426 & \$270 & 0.65 & Interior \\
    2026/27 & New & \$329 & \$325      & \$329 & \$329 & 0.54 & At cap \\
    \hline\hline
  \end{tabular}
  \smallskip

  \noindent\footnotesize
  \textit{Notes:} $\bar{p}$ is the VRR Point~(a) price cap. $\bar{p}_S$ is the
  Shapiro settlement cap (\$325/MW-day UCAP), applicable to the 2026/27 and
  2027/28 delivery years; ``---'' indicates the settlement does not apply.

The central finding of Table~\ref{tab:baseline} for the settlement evaluation
is that the Shapiro cap is \emph{binding}: the SFE equilibrium price for
2026/27, $p^* = \$329$/MW-day, exceeds the settlement cap $\bar{p}_S =
\$325$/MW-day. The gap is small at the RTO level (\$4/MW-day, roughly 1\%),
but it is structurally significant: it means that under the unmodified VRR
curve and $K = 3$ strategic sellers, equilibrium prices would settle at the
Point~(a) cap rather than the lower settlement cap. The settlement displaces
$\$4$/MW-day at the RTO level, but it does so by preventing the
capacity-constrained boundary from operating, eliminating the price signal
that would otherwise inform entry decisions. For constrained LDAs, the
binding gap is larger: 2025/26 LDA prices reached \$444--\$466/MW-day,
and the 2026/27 LDA equilibria likewise clear at or near $\bar{p}$ (see
Section~\ref{subsec:res_lda}), implying settlement displacement of
\$115--\$141/MW-day. These binding gaps are evaluated further in
Section~\ref{sec:21billion} against the \$21~billion savings claim.

The 2026/27 result is the most informative for identification. The Holmberg
boundary condition selects $p^* = \bar{p} = \$329.17$/MW-day for $K = 3$.
It is important to be transparent about what this means: the matching of the
observed clearing price is a \emph{mechanical} consequence of the boundary
condition, not an independent forecast. Once $K = 3$ is assumed and the
2026/27 capacity margin ($-0.2\%$) is tight enough to trigger the
at-cap regime, the ODE solution is forced to $\bar{p}$ by construction.
The dollar amount \$329.17 is simply the VRR Point~(a) price read from
the planning parameters. The substantively informative content is the
\emph{binary} at-cap versus interior outcome: for $K \geq 4$, the model
predicts interior clearing strictly below the cap (see
Section~\ref{subsec:res_concentration}), so the observed at-cap outcome
is consistent with $K \leq 3$ as the maximal effective seller count
in 2026/27. The model does not confirm the price; it confirms
the structural regime.

For 2023/24, the model predicts $p^* = \$330$ against an observed $p_{\text{actual}} = \$34$.
The divergence is consistent with effective offer price mitigation. Under PJM's
TPS rules, the three pivotal suppliers (RSI$_3 = 0.68$) had their offers capped
at approximately the ACR level, and with an over-supplied market (capacity
margin 9.9\%), the clearing price fell to \$34. The SFE $p^* = \$330$
represents the \emph{unmitigated} equilibrium --- the price that would prevail
if strategic sellers could bid without constraint. The 2025/26 gap ($p^* = \$426$
vs.\ $p_{\text{actual}} = \$270$) is smaller, reflecting the tighter market
conditions (capacity margin 1.6\%) and the price spike that occurred even under
partial mitigation.

Figure~\ref{fig:supply_functions} plots the equilibrium supply functions
$s(p)$ and the VRR demand curves $D(p)$ for each benchmark year. The supply
functions are solved by integrating the ODE~\eqref{eq:sfe_ode} backward from
the boundary condition at $\bar{p}$. All three supply functions are upward
sloping in the interior of $(c, \bar{p})$, consistent with the SFE
characterization. The equilibrium occurs where the aggregate supply
$3 \cdot s(p^*)$ plus fringe supply meets the VRR demand.

\subsection{Comparative Statics: Market Concentration}
\label{subsec:res_concentration}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Figure~\ref{fig:K_lerner} and Table~\ref{tab:K_sensitivity} report the
equilibrium price-cost margin $L$ as a function of the number of strategic sellers $K$,
holding calibration-year parameters fixed. The results reveal three qualitative
zones.

\end{figure}

\noindent\textbf{Zone 1 ($K \leq 3$): High strategic markup, price cap binding.}
For $K = 2$, the market clears at $\bar{p}$ in all three benchmark years,
yielding Lerner indices of 0.64--0.67. The at-cap outcome is also produced by
$K = 3$ in 2026/27 (where the VRR cap $\bar{p} = \$329$ is lower, meeting the
equilibrium supply function sooner). The transition from $K = 2$ to $K = 3$
has a modest effect on the price-cost margin in 2023/24 (0.64 to 0.55) but almost
none in 2025/26 (0.67 to 0.65), because in 2025/26 the supply function
remains close to the cap throughout.

\noindent\textbf{Zone 2 ($K = 4$): Moderate strategic markup.} Adding a fourth
seller reduces the price-cost margin by 33--45 percentage points relative to
$K = 3$, shifting the 2023/24 equilibrium from \$330 to \$215 and the 2026/27
equilibrium from the cap to \$228. The ODE~\eqref{eq:sfe_ode} makes this
intuitive: increasing $K$ divides the denominator, forcing $s'(p)$ to be
larger in absolute value at any given $(p, s)$ pair, which means firms must
offer more capacity at every price below the cap and the equilibrium supply
function shifts outward.

\noindent\textbf{Zone 3 ($K \geq 7$): Near-competitive.} With seven or more
strategic sellers, equilibrium prices fall to within 0.5\% of marginal cost
($c = \$150$/MW-day) in all three years. The price-cost margin is effectively zero
($L < 0.02$), consistent with the competitive limiting result
$p^* \to c$ as $K \to \infty$, implied by equation~\eqref{eq:sfe_ode}.

The structural implication is direct: the observed 2026/27 at-cap outcome
($p^* = \$329$) is consistent with $K \leq 3$ but inconsistent with $K \geq 4$
(which would predict interior clearing at \$228 or below). This makes 2026/27
a useful natural experiment for establishing the effective number of strategic
sellers.

\subsection{Comparative Statics: VRR Curve Design}
\label{subsec:res_vrr}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Figure~\ref{fig:vrr_slope} shows the price-cost margin $L$ as a function of the VRR
slope scaling factor. A factor of $f = 1$ corresponds to the baseline slope
for each year; $f < 1$ means a flatter VRR (less elastic demand) and $f > 1$
a steeper VRR (more elastic demand on the sloped segment).

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{../Figures/fig03_vrr_slope.pdf}
  \caption{Price-Cost Margin vs.\ VRR Slope Scaling Factor ($K = 3$)}
  \label{fig:vrr_slope}
  {\small\noindent\textit{Notes:} The slope scaling factor multiplies the quantity
    increment $(q_b - q_a)$ on the sloped segment, leaving the anchor prices
    fixed. Factor $= 1$ is the calibrated baseline.}
\end{figure}

The results confirm the prediction from Equation~\eqref{eq:sfe_ode}: steeper
VRR demand (larger $|D'(p)|$) reduces equilibrium markups by increasing the
$D'(p)$ term in the ODE numerator, forcing a larger $s'(p)$ and thus a more
competitive supply function. A doubling of the VRR slope reduces the Lerner
index by roughly 20--30 percentage points in the interior-clearing benchmark
years (2023/24 and 2025/26). For 2026/27 at $K = 3$, the outcome remains
at the price cap until the slope factor exceeds approximately 1.4, at which
point the supply function becomes steep enough to clear below the cap.

The 2026 VRR redesign (FERC Order ER25-1357) steepened the sloped segment
between $p_f$ and $\bar{p}$ by reducing the quantity width of that segment,
consistent with a slope scaling factor slightly above 1.0. In isolation, this
steepening would reduce markups. However, the new design also introduces a
flat floor at $p_f \approx \$177$/MW-day, which truncates the lower tail of the
price distribution and prevents equilibrium prices from falling below $p_f$
regardless of $K$. This floor effect dominates at high $K$: with $K = 5$ and
the new design, the minimum clearing price is \$177 (the floor), compared to
\$168 with the old design (which allows clearing down to $c = \$150$).

\subsection{Fringe Supply and Transmission Counterfactual}
\label{subsec:res_transmission}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Figure~\ref{fig:fringe} traces the price-cost margin $L$ as the fringe supply
$\bar{Q}_f$ varies from 70\% to 150\% of its calibrated baseline, holding
all other parameters fixed. The fringe captures competitive imports and
non-strategic capacity, so this comparative static approximates the effect of
expanding the Capacity Emergency Transfer Limit (CETL) or reducing
transmission congestion.

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{../Figures/fig04_fringe.pdf}
  \caption{Price-Cost Margin vs.\ Fringe Supply Scaling Factor ($K = 3$)}
  \label{fig:fringe}
  {\small\noindent\textit{Notes:} Fringe supply scaling factor of 1.0 equals the
    calibrated $\bar{Q}_f$ for each benchmark year. Fringe bids at $c = c_f$
    (same ACR floor). The fringe supply step function shifts the equilibrium
    clearing quantity without affecting the ODE itself.}
\end{figure}

Increasing fringe supply by 30\% (factor $= 1.3$) reduces the price-cost margin by
12--18 percentage points in the interior-clearing years, corresponding to
equilibrium price reductions of \$30--\$75/MW-day. The mechanism is
straightforward: more fringe capacity shifts the market-clearing condition
\eqref{eq:clearing_sym} rightward, requiring the strategic sellers to clear at
a lower point on their supply functions where the markup is smaller.

Notably, for 2026/27, expanding the fringe at $K = 3$ reduces the price-cost
margin from 0.54 to roughly 0.30 at factor $= 1.3$, which corresponds to a
price reduction from the cap (\$329) to approximately \$215/MW-day. This
suggests that expanding import capacity to the RTO would have a substantial
effect on equilibrium pricing even without structural remedies to seller
concentration. The implied cost-effectiveness of transmission investment versus
divestiture depends on the marginal cost per MW of CETL capacity --- a
comparison we leave for future work.

\subsection{LDA-Level Exposure to the Settlement Cap}
\label{subsec:res_lda}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The RTO-level analysis treats PJM as a single market, but capacity is traded
separately in constrained Locational Deliverability Areas. For each LDA, the
CETL imposes a hard upper bound on competitive imports, and local strategic
sellers cover the residual reliability requirement. This section applies the
same SFE model to each LDA individually, calibrating the competitive fringe at
$\bar{Q}_f = \text{CETL}$ and setting each strategic seller's capacity at
$\bar{q} = (\text{rel.~req.} - \text{CETL}) / K$.

Table~\ref{tab:lda_lerner} reports the implied Lerner indices for all LDAs
with valid CETL data across the three benchmark years. Two patterns dominate.

\input{../Paper/tables/tab_lda_lerner}

\noindent\textbf{Uniformly high price-cost margins.} Lerner indices range from
0.48 (DEOK in 2025/26) to 0.70 (PS and PS NORTH in 2025/26), with most
LDAs above 0.60 in every year. The elevated price-cost margins are
consistent with low import penetration ratios: across the sample,
$\text{CETL}/\text{rel.~req.}$ averages 0.40, meaning strategic sellers
must cover 60\% of the reliability requirement on average. In 2026/27,
every LDA clears at or within \$0.50 of the \$329.17 price cap ---
perfectly consistent with the RTO result and confirming that the at-cap
outcome is not an artifact of RTO aggregation.

\noindent\textbf{Transmission constraints amplify price-cost margins.}
Figure~\ref{fig:cetl_scatter} illustrates the relationship between import
penetration and the price-cost margin. The dashed trend lines (estimated
separately per year) are uniformly downward-sloping: LDAs with higher
CETL relative to their reliability requirement systematically exhibit lower
equilibrium markups. A 10-percentage-point increase in the import
penetration ratio is associated with a 4--6-point reduction in the
price-cost margin depending on the benchmark year. DEOK, which has the highest import
penetration ratio in the sample at 96\% (2025/26), is the only LDA to
produce an interior equilibrium price below the RTO price (\$290 vs.\
\$270).

These results have a direct policy implication. The CETL is the primary
instrument available to PJM for reducing local strategic pricing without
structural divestiture. The cross-LDA variation confirms the comparative
static from Section~\ref{subsec:res_transmission}: expanding the CETL is
quantitatively as important as reducing strategic concentration for LDAs
in the middle of the import-penetration distribution. For LDAs at the left
tail --- PS, PS NORTH, COMED, and EMAAC --- even large CETL expansions
would leave price-cost margins above 0.50 given the current number of
strategic sellers, indicating that structural remedies are necessary to
achieve competitive outcomes.
