\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\label{subsec:res_baseline}

from the 2025 IMM State of the Market). For each year, the table reports the
model-predicted equilibrium price $p^*$, the observed BRA clearing price, and
the implied price-cost margin (Lerner index) $L = (p^* - c)/p^*$.

The central finding is that the Shapiro cap is \emph{binding}: the SFE
equilibrium for 2026/27, $p^* = \$329$/MW-day, exceeds the settlement cap
$\bar{p}_S = \$325$/MW-day by \$4/MW-day at the RTO level. For constrained
LDAs, the binding gap is larger (\$115--\$141/MW-day based on 2025/26
clearing prices of \$444--\$466/MW-day; Section~\ref{subsec:res_lda}).

The 2026/27 result is the most informative for assessing the maintained model. The Holmberg
boundary condition selects $p^* = \bar{p} = \$329.17$/MW-day for $K = 3$.
It is important to be transparent about what this means: the matching of the
observed clearing price is a \emph{mechanical} consequence of the boundary
condition, not an independent forecast. Once $K = 3$ is assumed and the
2026/27 capacity margin ($-0.2\%$) is tight enough to trigger the
at-cap regime, the ODE solution is forced to $\bar{p}$ by construction.
The dollar amount \$329.17 is simply the VRR Point~(a) price read from
the planning parameters. The substantively informative content is the
\emph{binary} at-cap versus interior outcome: for $K \geq 4$, the model
predicts interior clearing strictly below the cap (see
Section~\ref{subsec:res_concentration}), so the observed at-cap outcome
is consistent with $K \leq 3$ as the maximal effective seller count
in 2026/27. The model does not confirm the price. It confirms
the structural regime.

For all five old-design years, $p^*$ substantially exceeds the observed
clearing price. The gap is largest in well-supplied years where TPS
mitigation was broadly applied (e.g., $p^* = \$330$ vs.\ $p_{\text{actual}}
= \$34$ in 2023/24, with RSI$_3 = 0.68$ and capacity margin 9.9\%).
The gap narrows in 2025/26 ($p^* = \$426$ vs.\ $p_{\text{actual}} = \$270$),
reflecting a capacity margin of only 1.6\% and a price spike that occurred
even under partial mitigation. This pattern is consistent across all
old-design years: the SFE predicts the \emph{unmitigated} equilibrium,
while actual prices reflect the TPS screen.

This distinction shapes how the 2026/27 and 2027/28 results should be read.
The SFE equilibrium $p^*$ describes what the auction would clear at under
strategic bidding when no binding mitigation cap is imposed. The observed
clearing price equals the minimum of $p^*$ and the TPS-mitigated offer cap
(approximately $c \cdot (1 + m)$, where $m$ is the IMM's mitigation
multiplier); in old-design years that minimum is the mitigation cap, not
the SFE. For 2026/27 and 2027/28 the $K = 3$ equilibrium is at the VRR
Point~(a) cap (\$329 and \$333), which sits below any plausible TPS
mitigation cap for combined-cycle-anchored capacity offers; the
settlement cap at \$325 therefore binds below both the SFE benchmark and
the TPS screen, and the counterfactual used for the \$612~million two-year
transfer (Section~\ref{sec:21billion}) uses the SFE rather than the TPS
mitigation cap. If TPS mitigation would have bound near \$325 in the
absence of the settlement---an empirical question that would require
endogenizing the TPS test---the SFE-based transfer estimate overstates
the true settlement-induced reduction in capacity payments.

Table~\ref{tab:cost_sensitivity} varies the assumed marginal cost $c$ from
\$100 to \$200 per MW-day. The at-cap finding is fully robust: $p^*$
exceeds the settlement cap for all cost assumptions shown. The Lerner
index in 2026/27 ranges from 0.39 (at $c = \$200$) to 0.70 (at
$c = \$100$); interior equilibria show limited price sensitivity to $c$.

For $K = 2$, the market clears at $\bar{p}$ in all seven delivery years,
yielding Lerner indices of 0.54--0.69. The at-cap outcome is also produced by
$K = 3$ in 2026/27 (where the VRR cap $\bar{p} = \$329$ is lower, meeting the
equilibrium supply function sooner). The transition from $K = 2$ to $K = 3$
has a modest effect on the price-cost margin in 2023/24 (0.64 to 0.55) but almost
none in 2025/26 (0.67 to 0.65), because in 2025/26 the supply function
remains close to the cap throughout.
Adding a fourth seller reduces the price-cost margin by 33--45 percentage
points relative to $K = 3$, shifting the 2023/24 equilibrium from \$330 to
\$215 and the 2026/27 equilibrium from the cap to \$228. The
ODE~\eqref{eq:sfe_ode} makes this intuitive: increasing $K$ divides the
denominator, forcing $s'(p)$ to be larger in absolute value at any given
$(p, s)$ pair, which means firms must offer more capacity at every price
below the cap and the equilibrium supply function shifts outward.
With seven or more strategic sellers, equilibrium prices fall to within
0.5\% of marginal cost ($c = \$150$/MW-day) in all seven years, with price-cost
margins effectively zero ($L < 0.02$), consistent with the competitive
limiting result $p^* \to c$ as $K \to \infty$ implied by
equation~\eqref{eq:sfe_ode}.

The structural implication is direct: the observed 2026/27 at-cap outcome
($p^* = \$329$) is consistent with $K \leq 3$ but inconsistent with $K \geq 4$
(which would predict interior clearing at \$228 or below). This is a
model-based inference, not an identification result: under the maintained SFE
specification, $K \geq 4$ cannot rationalize the at-cap regime, but $K$ itself
is not identified from the auction data.

\label{subsec:res_vrr}

Figure~\ref{fig:vrr_slope} shows the price-cost margin $L$ as a function of the VRR
slope scaling factor ($f = 1$ is the baseline; $f > 1$ is steeper demand).

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{../Figures/fig03_vrr_slope.pdf}
  \caption{Steeper VRR Demand Reduces Markups and Can Eliminate Cap-Binding}
  \label{fig:vrr_slope}
  {\small\noindent\textit{Notes:} Source: SFE model with slope factor applied to
    the VRR quantity increment $(q_b - q_a)$, anchor prices held fixed.
    Factor $= 1$ is the calibrated baseline.}
\end{figure}

Steeper VRR demand reduces equilibrium markups by increasing the $D'(p)$
term in the ODE, forcing a more competitive supply function. A doubling of
the slope reduces the Lerner index by 20--30 percentage points in interior-clearing
years. For 2026/27 at $K = 3$, the outcome remains at the price cap until
the slope factor exceeds approximately 1.4, at which point the supply
function clears below the cap without an external price constraint.

\label{subsec:res_transmission}

Figure~\ref{fig:fringe} traces the price-cost margin $L$ as fringe supply
$\bar{Q}_f$ varies from 70\% to 150\% of its calibrated baseline. The
fringe captures competitive imports and non-strategic capacity, so this
comparative static approximates the effect of expanding CETL or reducing
transmission congestion.

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{../Figures/fig04_fringe.pdf}
  \caption{Expanding Competitive Fringe Substantially Reduces Markups}
  \label{fig:fringe}
  {\small\noindent\textit{Notes:} Source: SFE model with fringe supply $\bar{Q}_f$
    scaled from 70\% to 150\% of the RSI-derived calibrated baseline for each
    benchmark year.}
\end{figure}

A 30\% increase in fringe supply reduces the price-cost margin by 12--18
percentage points in interior-clearing years. For 2026/27, expanding the
fringe at $K = 3$ reduces the margin from 0.54 to roughly 0.30 (a price
drop from \$329 to approximately \$215/MW-day), suggesting that expanding
import capacity would substantially affect equilibrium pricing even without
structural remedies to seller concentration.

\label{subsec:res_lda}

Capacity is traded separately in constrained LDAs. For each LDA, I
calibrate $\bar{Q}_f = \text{CETL}$ and set each strategic seller's capacity
at $\bar{q} = (\text{rel.~req.} - \text{CETL}) / K$.
Table~\ref{tab:lda_lerner} reports the implied Lerner indices for all LDAs
with valid CETL data.

\input{../Paper/tables/tab_lda_lerner}

Lerner indices range from 0.48 (DEOK in 2025/26) to 0.70 (PS and PS NORTH
in 2025/26), with most LDAs above 0.60 in every year. In 2026/27, every LDA
clears at or within \$0.50 of the price cap, confirming that the at-cap
outcome is not an artifact of RTO aggregation.
Figure~\ref{fig:cetl_scatter} shows that LDAs with higher import penetration
($\text{CETL}/\text{rel.~req.}$) systematically exhibit lower markups: a
10-percentage-point increase in import penetration is associated with a
4--6-point reduction in the price-cost margin.
