\section{Allocative Implications of the Price Band}
\label{sec:allocative_cost}

The settlement cap's allocative significance depends on its proximity to the
investment threshold. In 2026/27, Net CONE is \$212.14/MW-day; the settlement
cap at \$325/MW-day exceeds it by 53\%, so the cap alone does not prevent new
entry from being financially viable. The more substantive concern is
informational: because the cap binds in consecutive years (2026/27 and
2027/28), potential entrants observe the cap rather than the unconstrained
equilibrium price and cannot distinguish between a market that would have
cleared at \$333 and one that would have cleared far above \$325. This
uncertainty reduces the informativeness of the capacity price for investment
decisions \citep{Anderson2005_sfe_pricecaps}, a concern amplified in
constrained LDAs where local Net CONE values exceed the settlement cap
(e.g., PS and PS NORTH Net CONE $= \$369$/MW-day in 2026/27).

The \$175/MW-day floor operates differently: it prevents price collapse in
over-supplied conditions, functioning as an investment adequacy mechanism
rather than a cost-reduction measure \citep{PJM2025_settlement_slides}. At
the RTO level, the floor falls below Net CONE (\$212.14) and does not
guarantee full cost recovery. Combined with the cap, the settlement creates
a price band (\$175--\$325) that is neither the competitive equilibrium
($c \approx \$150$) nor the SFE benchmark ($p^* \approx$ \$329--\$333).
For $K = 3$, the SFE equilibrium falls above the band, so the cap truncates
the price signal rather than confirming it. A formal welfare accounting---
comparing deadweight losses from the truncated investment signal against
consumer savings from lower cleared prices---is outside the scope of this
paper; the observation here is that the cap is close enough to Net CONE to
leave entry incentives broadly intact, and the remaining allocative concern
is informational rather than direct cost recovery.
