\section{Evaluating the \$21~Billion Claim}
\label{sec:21billion}

\label{subsec:21b_counterfactual}

Pennsylvania's January 28, 2025 announcement stated that the settlement
would save consumers ``over \$21~billion over the next two delivery years''
\citep{Shapiro2025_press_release}. The arithmetic is straightforward:
(counterfactual price $-$ \$325) $\times$ cleared quantity $\times$ 2 years.
Shapiro's stated counterfactual is ``over \$500/MW-Day,'' which represents
the projected VRR Point~(a) price under the pre-settlement calibration
methodology \citep{PJM2025_settlement_slides}. But the VRR Point~(a) price
is not a strategic equilibrium price. It is the maximum of the
administrative demand schedule: the price at which the VRR curve becomes
perfectly inelastic. In equilibrium, the capacity market clears where supply
meets the VRR schedule, which, as Section~\ref{sec:results} shows, may occur
anywhere in the range $[c, \bar{p}]$ depending on market structure. The VRR
counterfactual therefore uses an administrative ceiling rather than a
market-clearing benchmark.

The SFE model provides a structural alternative: $p^*$, the equilibrium
price under strategic bidding against the \emph{unmodified} VRR curve. In
2026/27, $p^* = \$329.17$/MW-day; in 2027/28, $p^* = \$333.44$/MW-day.
Both equilibria fall at the respective VRR Point~(a) caps because the
Holmberg boundary condition is active for $K \leq 3$, but they are
substantially below the \$500 figure in Shapiro's claim.

Using \$500/MW-day as the counterfactual and RTO-level quantities, the
two-year revenue transfer is \$17.16~billion. The stated \$21~billion figure
includes contributions from constrained LDAs. The structural SFE estimate
implies a two-year revenue transfer of approximately \$612~million at the
RTO level---roughly 35~times smaller---because the 2026/27 strategic
equilibrium falls near the design ceiling, not because the settlement
eliminates large rents. The two counterfactuals differ not because one is
wrong, but because they answer different questions: the VRR counterfactual
measures the gap between the design-level cap and the settlement cap, while
the SFE counterfactual measures the reduction in the predicted clearing
price attributable to the price band.

\label{subsec:21b_limitations}

The findings rest on maintained assumptions whose validity cannot be fully
tested. First, I assume $K = 3$ symmetric sellers with identical costs.
Actual PJM suppliers differ in technology, cost structure, and contract
positions, so the symmetric Lerner index should be interpreted as a central
estimate, not a precise measurement. Second, the Holmberg boundary condition
is derived for stochastic-demand markets; in the deterministic BRA, it is
imposed as an equilibrium selection criterion, and alternative selection
rules would yield different equilibrium prices. Third, the static model
treats capacity as exogenous and cannot quantify entry distortions from the
settlement. A complete savings calculation should account for retirements
accelerated by lower prices and entry decisions informed by the observed
2026/27 and 2027/28 clearing prices; the \$21~billion figure and the SFE
estimate both hold quantity fixed. Fourth, even if greenfield entry is
infeasible within the 10-month lead time of the 2026/27 BRA, auction prices
inform decisions beyond direct construction---retirement deterrence, demand
response registration, bilateral contracting, and forward expectations for
2028/29 and beyond \citep{Cramton2005_capacity}---so the informational cost
of suppressing the price signal extends beyond the settlement's two-year
window. Fifth, I do not model TPS mitigation endogenously; the gap between
predicted and actual prices in old-design years suggests mitigation effects
the model does not capture. If TPS mitigation would have produced offer
caps near the settlement cap in 2026/27 absent the settlement, the
SFE-based transfer of \$612~million overstates the settlement's true
reduction in payments; the observed old-design gap between $p^*$ and
$p_{\text{actual}}$ cannot be used to rule this out, because TPS screening
in those years occurred with capacity margins of 9.9\% and 11.7\% where the
test was much more likely to be triggered than in the at-cap 2026/27 and
2027/28 auctions, where RSI$_3$ was binding but TPS application is
regime-dependent. These limitations bound the precision of the quantitative
estimates but do not affect the qualitative finding that the settlement cap
is binding under concentrated supply.
