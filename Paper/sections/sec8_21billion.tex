\section{Evaluating the \$21~Billion Claim}
\label{sec:21billion}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Counterfactual Behind the Claim}
\label{subsec:21b_counterfactual}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Pennsylvania's January 28, 2025 announcement stated that the settlement
would save consumers ``over \$21~billion over the next two delivery years''
\citep{Shapiro2025_press_release}. The arithmetic behind this figure is
straightforward: (counterfactual price $-$ \$325) $\times$ cleared quantity
$\times$ 2 delivery years. Shapiro's stated counterfactual is ``over
\$500/MW-Day'' \citep{PJM2025_settlement_slides}. This figure represents the
projected VRR Point~(a) price for 2026/27 under the \emph{pre-settlement}
calibration methodology, which relied on the Gross CONE of the dual-fuel
combustion turbine reference technology before netting out projected energy
and ancillary service revenues. Under the pre-reform methodology, a higher
Gross CONE would have produced a Point~(a) substantially above the
\$329.17 produced by the new-design calibration. The 2025/26 Point~(a) of
\$451.61 (Table~\ref{tab:vrr_params}) is consistent with this range; the
complaint projected a further increase to ``over \$500'' for 2026/27 absent
reform.

This counterfactual deserves scrutiny. The VRR Point~(a) price is not a
strategic equilibrium price. It is the maximum of the administrative demand
schedule: the price at which the VRR curve becomes perfectly inelastic (the
``flat top'' in the old design). In equilibrium, the capacity market clears
where supply meets the VRR schedule --- which, as Section~\ref{sec:results}
shows, may occur anywhere in the range $[c, \bar{p}]$ depending on market
structure. For $K = 3$, the model predicts interior equilibria in 2023/24 and
2025/26 that fall substantially below Point~(a). The VRR counterfactual therefore uses an administrative ceiling rather than
a market-clearing benchmark: the Point~(a) price is the top anchor of the
demand schedule, not a predicted equilibrium price. The SFE model provides
a more disciplined market-clearing comparison under the model's assumptions,
computing where the unmodified demand schedule would have cleared under
strategic bidding.

Our SFE model provides a structural alternative counterfactual: $p^*$, the
equilibrium price under strategic bidding against the \emph{unmodified} VRR
curve. This is what the market would have cleared at absent the settlement,
given the observed market structure. In 2026/27, $p^* = \$329.17$/MW-day;
in 2027/28, $p^* = \$333.44$/MW-day (the observed BRA clearing price, consistent with the Holmberg at-cap condition; \citealt{PJM2025_settlement_slides}).
Both equilibria fall at the respective VRR Point~(a) caps because the
Holmberg boundary condition is active for $K \leq 3$, but they are
substantially below the \$500 figure in Shapiro's claim.

Several observations follow from Table~\ref{tab:21billion}.

\textbf{The VRR counterfactual overstates the settlement's direct benefit.}
Using \$500/MW-day as the counterfactual and RTO-level quantities, the
two-year revenue transfer is \$17.16~billion --- approximately 18\% below
the stated \$21~billion. The gap arises primarily from constrained LDAs,
where the settlement cap applies to the separately-clearing LDA demand
curves. In 2025/26, PJM's constrained LDA prices reached
\$444--\$466/MW-day; without the settlement, 2026/27 and 2027/28 LDA
equilibria in the tightest zones (PS, PS~NORTH, EMAAC) would likely have
cleared near their respective VRR caps as well, contributing additional
revenue transfer not captured in the RTO-only calculation.

\textbf{The SFE counterfactual yields a far smaller transfer.}
The structural estimate based on $p^*$ implies a two-year revenue transfer
of approximately \$612~million --- 35~times smaller than the \$21~billion
VRR-based claim. This does not mean the settlement is inconsequential: the
SFE and VRR counterfactuals differ not because one is wrong, but because
they answer different questions. The VRR counterfactual measures the gap
between the design-level cap and the settlement cap --- a policy decision
about where to set the demand curve ceiling. The SFE counterfactual
measures the gap between the model-predicted equilibrium price and the settlement cap
--- the reduction in the predicted clearing price attributable to the price band. The
first gap (\$175/MW-day) is much larger than the second (\$4--\$8/MW-day)
because the model-predicted equilibrium in 2026/27 falls near the VRR Point~(a) price.

\textbf{The competitive benchmark inverts the direction of transfer.}
If capacity markets achieved the competitive equilibrium ($c \approx
\$150$/MW-day), the settlement cap at \$325 would represent a transfer
\emph{to} sellers, not buyers --- \$175/MW-day above marginal cost.
This is unsurprising: capacity markets are \emph{designed} to clear above
marginal cost to provide investment signals \citep{Cramton2005_capacity,
Joskow2008_capacity_payments}. The relevant benchmark for evaluating
Shapiro's savings claim is not competitive pricing but rather the strategic
equilibrium, which is the price that would prevail in the market as
structured.

\subsection{Quantity Assumption and Supply Response}
\label{subsec:21b_quantity}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------

All three scenarios in Table~\ref{tab:21billion} hold quantity fixed at the
observed BRA clearing. This assumption is internally consistent for short-run
analysis --- the capacity that cleared in 2026/27 and 2027/28 was the supply
available given the lead times involved --- but it suppresses supply response.
PJM's settlement framing acknowledges this implicitly: the \$175 floor is
described as ``supporting near-term investment,'' recognizing that capping the
price without a floor could accelerate retirements and reduce future clearing
quantities.

A complete savings calculation should account for the effect of the price cap
on (i) retirement decisions for marginal capacity that would have been
retained under higher prices, and (ii) new entry decisions for the 2028/29
auction and beyond, which are informed by the observed 2026/27 and 2027/28
clearing prices. Both effects reduce future cleared quantities, reducing the
denominator of the cost-per-MW calculation. The \$21~billion figure holds
quantity fixed, so it overstates consumer savings to the extent the cap
depresses future entry and accelerates retirements. The SFE estimate
represents a more conservative counterfactual revenue-transfer under the
model's short-run assumptions: it uses a lower counterfactual price
(\$329 rather than \$500) and the same fixed-quantity assumption, so it
should be read as a calibrated benchmark rather than a bound.
