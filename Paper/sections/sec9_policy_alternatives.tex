\section{Implications for Market Design}
\label{sec:policy_alternatives}

This paper's primary contribution is evaluative rather than prescriptive:
the SFE model is suited to identifying the channel through which the
settlement affects prices and the structural conditions that support
cap-binding outcomes, not to constructing a welfare ranking of reforms.
Nonetheless, the channel analysis has implications for market design. The
settlement reduces equilibrium prices through direct truncation of the VRR
clearing price; the model identifies three structural channels through which
prices might fall without that truncation.

\paragraph{Transmission expansion and competitive fringe.}
The fringe-size comparative static (Section~\ref{subsec:res_transmission}) shows
that a 30\% increase in competitive fringe supply at $K = 3$ reduces the
price-cost margin from 0.54 to approximately 0.30 in 2026/27, operating
through market structure rather than the demand curve. The LDA cross-section
reinforces this channel: a 10-percentage-point increase in import
penetration is associated with a 4--6-point reduction in the local price-cost
margin. The relevant regulatory instrument is PJM's Regional Transmission
Expansion Plan directed toward import-constrained LDAs \citep{PJM_RTEP}.

\paragraph{VRR curve recalibration.}
Steepening the VRR demand curve reduces equilibrium markups through the ODE
mechanism: larger $|D'(p)|$ disciplines the supply function at every price
below the cap. Under the model, a slope scaling factor of approximately 1.4
or greater would shift the 2026/27 outcome from cap-binding to
interior-clearing at $K = 3$, allowing prices to settle below Point~(a)
without an external cap. FERC Order ER25-1357 modified the curve's shape in this general direction,
though primarily via cap/floor truncation rather than pure slope change
\citep{FERC2025_ER25-1357}; further recalibration would reduce equilibrium
prices through the demand-elasticity channel rather than through price
truncation.

\paragraph{Entry and deconcentration.}
The transition from cap-binding to interior equilibria occurs between
$K = 3$ and $K = 4$: adding a fourth symmetric strategic seller moves the
2026/27 equilibrium from \$329 to \$228 (Section~\ref{subsec:res_concentration}),
a \$101/MW-day reduction without any modification of the demand curve. The
relevant regulatory levers include interconnection queue reform
\citep{FERC2023_order2023, FERC2024_order2023A} and expanded demand response
and battery storage participation.

Under the model's assumptions, each structural channel produces a reduction
in the SFE markup by altering concentration or fringe supply rather than
truncating the price signal. Whether any of these changes is warranted
depends on regulatory objectives, cost--benefit considerations, and political
economy factors outside the model. The model's contribution is to identify
the mechanism: the settlement truncates the clearing price at the Point~(a)
boundary; structural changes to the seller side or the demand schedule would
alter the conditions that place the equilibrium there.
