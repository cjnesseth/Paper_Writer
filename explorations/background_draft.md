# Section 2: Background

## 2.1 The Rise of Data Center Alley

Loudoun County, Virginia — specifically the Ashburn corridor — is the largest data center market in the world. The concentration traces to a single infrastructure decision: in 1992, the Metropolitan Area Ethernet exchange point MAE-East was established near Dulles International Airport, making Ashburn one of only four original internet exchange nodes in the United States. The low-latency advantage of co-location near this exchange attracted the first wave of internet companies in the mid-1990s. When the dot-com bubble burst in 2001, many facilities stood vacant, but the underlying fiber infrastructure remained, and the corridor proved durable. Recovery was gradual through the 2000s and then accelerated sharply as cloud computing scaled.

By the early 2010s, Ashburn had earned the informal designation "Data Center Alley." Today Loudoun County is home to approximately 200 data centers — more than any other jurisdiction on earth — and as of 2024 had permitted 43 million square feet of data center space, a 231 percent increase over five years (Loudoun County 2024). Northern Virginia as a whole had surpassed 4,900 megawatts (MW) of operational data center capacity by Q1 2025, with Loudoun County accounting for roughly half of the state's data centers by number of sites, square footage, and energy usage (JLARC 2024). An additional 1,100 MW was under construction and more than 5,500 MW in active planning (Baxtel 2025). Hyperscale facilities — those operated by Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, and Meta — held approximately 53 percent of the Northern Virginia market share in 2025. The planned pipeline would add 24 million more square feet within the county over the next eight years.

## 2.2 Fiscal Benefits: Data Centers as the County's Tax Base

The fiscal transformation of Loudoun County is striking. The county levies a Business Personal Property (BPP) tax on the computer equipment and servers housed in data centers. Because this equipment depreciates rapidly and turns over frequently, it generates large and growing annual assessments without the permanence of real property.

The trajectory of BPP and real property tax revenues from data centers illustrates the county's dependence on the sector:

- FY2016: approximately $146 million in combined data center tax revenue
- FY2020: approximately $330 million (largely BPP on equipment)
- FY2022: $663 million total
- FY2025: data centers contribute more than $1 billion in annual tax revenue — roughly one-third of the county's entire operating budget (Loudoun County 2024)
- FY2026 (projected): approximately $1.37 billion from BPP on computer equipment alone (NetChoice 2024)

This revenue has allowed the Board of Supervisors to reduce the residential real property tax rate every year for more than a decade, from $1.145 per $100 assessed value in tax year 2016 to $0.805 in 2025 — a cumulative reduction that officials estimate saves the average Loudoun homeowner $3,000–$4,000 per year (Loudoun County 2024). The December 2024 JLARC report estimated that data centers generate $700 million in local tax revenue to Loudoun County, 31 percent of its annual budget, and contribute $9.1 billion to Virginia GDP and 74,000 jobs (largely construction) statewide annually (JLARC 2024).

Crucially, Virginia provides data centers a sales and use tax exemption on equipment purchases (Va. Code § 58.1-609.3), which functions as a state-level incentive. Unlike many other jurisdictions nationally (see Jaros 2025), Loudoun County does not provide locally negotiated tax abatements on BPP. The state-level exemption forgoes roughly $1 billion per year in state revenue (JLARC 2024; Governing 2023), but the county still captures the full annual BPP levy on assessed equipment value.

## 2.3 Zoning and Regulatory Milestones

Data centers have historically been permitted by right in Loudoun County's industrial and planned development zones (PD-IP and similar districts). The absence of a special-use or conditional-use permit requirement meant approvals were administrative — handled by staff without Board review or public hearings. This expedited process was a competitive advantage: developers could secure permits quickly.

The regulatory environment shifted decisively between 2023 and 2025 as community opposition intensified:

- **December 2023:** Loudoun County adopted a new comprehensive Zoning Ordinance. A proposed overhead transmission line traversing active farmlands, wetlands, and a historic village — necessitated by data center load growth — galvanized opposition groups and raised the political salience of data center externalities.
- **2024:** The Board of Supervisors denied a major data center application for the first time. On July 2, 2024, the Board approved an Intent to Amend the Zoning Ordinance, launching a formal process to restrict by-right data center approvals.
- **March 18, 2025:** The Board of Supervisors approved a Comprehensive Plan amendment and Zoning Ordinance amendment eliminating by-right data center development across the county. Data centers now require a Special Exception — a legislative review with public hearings before both the Planning Commission and the Board of Supervisors (Holland & Knight 2025; Loudoun Now 2025). Existing applications filed before the effective date were grandfathered.
- **Note on moratorium:** Virginia law does not permit a blanket moratorium on new construction; each application must be considered on its individual merits (Loudoun County FAQ). The Special Exception process achieves similar practical effect through case-by-case review.

In early 2026, the Virginia General Assembly considered multiple bills to reshape data center regulation at the state level, including provisions addressing siting, ratepayer cost allocation, and tax treatment (VPM 2026).

## 2.4 Electricity Infrastructure and Rate Incidence

Data centers are among the most electricity-intensive industrial facilities. Northern Virginia data centers require approximately 4,140 MW of electricity, according to the December 2024 JLARC report — nearly equivalent to a dedicated regional grid. Dominion Energy Virginia serves the overwhelming majority of this load.

Dominion's capital investment plans reflect the scale of the challenge. The utility projected total capital expenditure of $50.1 billion between 2025 and 2029, up from a prior estimate of $43.2 billion — a difference largely driven by data center load growth (Dominion 2025). Its 2024 Integrated Resource Plan forecast approximately $22 billion in data-center-driven costs over 15 years, including 89 discrete transmission line projects required solely to serve new data center demand. The transmission component alone carries an estimated $28.3 billion in capital expense (Dominion 2025 IRP).

These infrastructure costs flow back to ratepayers through the utility's rate structure. The State Corporation Commission approved a new GS-5 rate class in 2025, effective January 1, 2027, covering large-scale customers demanding 25 MW or more. Under GS-5, customers must pay a minimum of 85 percent of contracted distribution and transmission demand and 60 percent of contracted generation demand — a structure designed to improve cost-causation alignment. However, the JLARC (2024) report found that data centers' increased demand will likely increase system costs for all customers, including residential ratepayers who are not directly responsible for the load growth. The precise per-household rate impact depends on cost allocation assumptions that are actively contested in SCC proceedings.

## 2.5 The Political Economy of Siting: Why Loudoun Is a Canonical Case

Loudoun County is an unusually clean natural laboratory for studying the political economy of infrastructure siting. Several features distinguish it:

1. **Concentrated fiscal benefits:** With data centers funding roughly one-third of the county budget and enabling residential tax rate reductions, the fiscal benefit is large, observable, and widely publicized by county officials.

2. **Concentrated local costs:** Data centers cluster in specific corridors (Ashburn, Sterling, Leesburg), creating highly localized noise, visual, and traffic externalities while the tax benefits diffuse across all county residents.

3. **Regulatory transition:** The shift from by-right approvals to Special Exception in March 2025 provides a policy discontinuity. Post-2025 applications face substantially higher political and procedural barriers.

4. **Rich data environment:** Loudoun's open data portal, Commissioner of Revenue records, and parcel GIS data provide the inputs needed for a property-value hedonic analysis that is not available for most jurisdictions.

The policy question this paper addresses — whether the concentrated fiscal benefits to the county as a whole justify the concentrated costs to nearby residents — maps directly onto the NIMBY framework developed by Jarvis (2025) for renewable energy. The key political economy distortion in Jarvis is that local officials overweight property value costs borne by politically vocal nearby residents relative to broader social benefits. In Loudoun, the analogous distortion runs in the *opposite direction*: county officials may have historically underweighted localized resident costs (noise, aesthetics, traffic, property value effects) relative to the substantial and visible fiscal benefits that funded tax rate reductions benefiting all homeowners. The 2025 regulatory tightening suggests this balance has begun to shift.
