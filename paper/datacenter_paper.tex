\begin{abstract}
\noindent Loudoun County, Virginia hosts approximately 200 data centers---more than any other jurisdiction in the world---generating over \$895 million in annual tax revenue and funding a 30 percent residential property tax rate reduction since 2012. I examine the distributional incidence of this concentration through three channels: property values, tax revenue, and electricity rates. Using a \citet{SunAbraham2021} cohort-robust event study with 31,333 resale transactions (2020--2025) and staggered data center permit dates as treatment events, I estimate an average treatment effect on the treated of $-1.3$ percent ($p < 0.01$). A TWFE ring specification finds statistically significant effects of $-3.1$ percent at 1--2 km and $-1.6$ percent at 2--4 km. However, results are sensitive to specification: the 0--1 km ring is imprecisely estimated, the event study does not show a monotonically increasing post-treatment pattern, and a cumulative exposure measure yields near-zero coefficients. An upper-bound estimate of the annual tax savings attributable to data center revenue is \$2,512 per homeowner, against an electricity rate increase of \$168--\$444 per residential customer. These diffuse benefits and costs accrue to different populations than the spatially concentrated property value effects, complicating welfare comparisons. I argue that Loudoun's 2025 shift from by-right to discretionary data center approval is best understood through a \citet{CalabresiMelamed1972} framework: the prior regime assigned a de facto property rule to developers while failing to internalize localized harms captured by neither the county's fiscal accounts nor the utility's rate structure.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ==========================================================================

Loudoun County, Virginia is the largest data center market in the world. Its approximately 200 data center facilities generate an estimated \$895 million in annual tax revenue---roughly one-third of the county's entire operating budget---and have enabled the Board of Supervisors to reduce the residential real property tax rate every year for more than a decade, from \$1.145 per \$100 of assessed value in 2012 to \$0.805 in 2025. Yet on March 18, 2025, the same Board voted to eliminate by-right data center development across the county, requiring each new facility to obtain a Special Exception through public hearings. What explains this reversal?

This paper investigates the distributional incidence of data center development through three channels. First, I use a hedonic difference-in-differences (DiD) design to estimate the association between data center proximity and residential property values. Using 31,333 arm's-length residential resale transactions from 2020 to 2025, I exploit staggered data center permit dates as treatment events and employ the \citet{SunAbraham2021} cohort-robust estimator to address concerns about heterogeneous treatment effects in staggered settings. The Sun-Abraham average treatment effect on the treated (ATT) is $-1.3$ percent ($p < 0.01$), and a TWFE ring specification estimates significant effects of $-3.1$ percent at 1--2 km ($p < 0.01$) and $-1.6$ percent at 2--4 km ($p < 0.01$). The evidence is suggestive of localized disamenity effects, though results are sensitive to specification choice and the 0--1 km ring is imprecisely estimated.

Second, I document the fiscal benefit channel: the annual residential tax savings attributable to data center revenue amount to approximately \$2,512 per homeowner at the median assessed value, using the 2012 rate as an upper-bound counterfactual. Third, I estimate the electricity rate incidence: data-center-driven transmission and distribution capital expenditure by Dominion Energy Virginia imposes an estimated \$168--\$444 per year on each residential customer across the utility's service territory \citep{JLARC2024}.

A central challenge for welfare analysis is that these three channels affect different populations. Tax savings accrue to Loudoun County homeowners (${\sim}130{,}000$ households). Electricity costs are distributed across Dominion's entire residential customer base (${\sim}2.36$ million customers). Property value effects are spatially concentrated among the approximately 3,236 parcels within 1 km and 12,513 parcels within 2 km of a data center. Comparing these channels as a ``net welfare'' calculation would require strong assumptions about commensurability that the data do not support. Instead, I present a partial incidence table that makes transparent who bears each cost and benefit.

From a law-and-economics perspective, Loudoun's regulatory transition provides a natural case study in entitlement allocation. The prior by-right regime functioned as a de facto property rule in the \citet{CalabresiMelamed1972} sense: developers held the entitlement to build, and neighbors bore the costs unless they could organize politically to block projects---a high-transaction-cost outcome. The Special Exception regime shifts toward a liability-rule framework in which the county screens each project for localized harms. I argue that the fiscal externality problem---in which the county government captures BPP tax revenue while some harms spill onto nearby homeowners and broader electricity ratepayers---created a systematic incentive to over-approve under the by-right regime, and that discretionary review may improve welfare allocation even if it raises development costs.

The paper contributes to several literatures. It provides the first quasi-experimental estimate of data center proximity effects on residential property values, extending the infrastructure disamenity literature on power plants \citep{Davis2011}, toxic facilities \citep{Currie2015}, shale gas \citep{Muehlenbachs2015}, and wind turbines \citep{Gibbons2015, Jarvis2025}. It contributes to the fiscal impact literature \citep{Greenstone2010, Bartik2020, Slattery2020} by documenting the unusually large tax base contribution of data centers under Virginia's BPP structure. And it connects the empirical evidence to a law-and-economics analysis of zoning entitlements, building on \citet{Fischel2001} and \citet{CalabresiMelamed1972}.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background} provides institutional background. Section~\ref{sec:literature} reviews the relevant literatures. Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the data. Section~\ref{sec:strategy} presents the empirical strategy. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses distributional incidence and the legal regime. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
% ==========================================================================

\subsection{The Rise of Data Center Alley}

Loudoun County, Virginia---specifically the Ashburn corridor---is the largest data center market in the world. The concentration traces to a single infrastructure decision: in 1992, the Metropolitan Area Ethernet exchange point MAE-East was established near Dulles International Airport, making Ashburn one of only four original internet exchange nodes in the United States. The low-latency advantage of co-location near this exchange attracted the first wave of internet companies in the mid-1990s. When the dot-com bubble burst in 2001, many facilities stood vacant, but the underlying fiber infrastructure remained, and the corridor proved durable. Recovery was gradual through the 2000s and then accelerated sharply as cloud computing scaled.

By the early 2010s, Ashburn had earned the informal designation ``Data Center Alley.'' Today Loudoun County is home to approximately 200 data centers---more than any other jurisdiction on earth---and as of 2024 had permitted 43 million square feet of data center space, a 231 percent increase over five years. Northern Virginia as a whole had surpassed 4,900 megawatts (MW) of operational data center capacity by Q1 2025, with Loudoun County accounting for roughly half of the state's data centers by number of sites, square footage, and energy usage \citep{JLARC2024}. Hyperscale facilities---those operated by Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, and Meta---held approximately 53 percent of the Northern Virginia market share in 2025.


\subsection{Fiscal Benefits: Data Centers as the County's Tax Base}

The fiscal transformation of Loudoun County is striking. The county levies a Business Personal Property (BPP) tax on the computer equipment and servers housed in data centers. Because this equipment depreciates rapidly and turns over frequently, it generates large and growing annual assessments.

The trajectory of data center tax revenues illustrates the county's dependence on the sector: from approximately \$146 million in FY2016 to \$423 million in FY2020, \$663 million in FY2022, \$733 million in FY2023, and \$875 million in FY2024 \citep{JLARC2024}. By FY2025, data centers contributed an estimated \$895 million in annual tax revenue. The JLARC report estimated that data centers contribute \$9.1 billion to Virginia GDP and support 74,000 jobs (largely construction) statewide annually.

This revenue has allowed the Board of Supervisors to reduce the residential real property tax rate every year for more than a decade, from \$1.145 per \$100 assessed value in tax year 2012 to \$0.805 in 2025. Crucially, unlike many other jurisdictions nationally \citep{Jaros2025}, Loudoun County does not provide locally negotiated tax abatements on BPP, though Virginia provides a state-level sales and use tax exemption on equipment purchases that forgoes roughly \$1 billion per year in state revenue \citep{JLARC2024, GoodJobsFirst2025}.


\subsection{Zoning: From By-Right to Special Exception}

Data centers had historically been permitted by right in Loudoun County's industrial and planned development zones. The absence of a special-use or conditional-use permit requirement meant approvals were administrative---handled by staff without Board review or public hearings.

The regulatory environment shifted decisively between 2023 and 2025 as community opposition intensified. A proposed overhead transmission line traversing farmlands, wetlands, and a historic village---necessitated by data center load growth---galvanized opposition groups. In 2024, the Board denied a major data center application for the first time. On March 18, 2025, the Board approved a Comprehensive Plan amendment eliminating by-right data center development. Data centers now require a Special Exception---a legislative review with public hearings. This shift in the legal entitlement structure is the focus of the law-and-economics analysis in Section~\ref{sec:legal}.


\subsection{Electricity Infrastructure and Rate Incidence}

Northern Virginia data centers require approximately 4,140 MW of electricity \citep{JLARC2024}. Dominion Energy Virginia projected \$22 billion in data-center-driven transmission and distribution costs over 15 years. The State Corporation Commission approved a new GS-5 rate class in 2025, effective January 2027, covering customers demanding 25 MW or more.\footnote{Virginia SCC, Final Order in Case No.\ PUR-2025-00058, November 25, 2025.} Under GS-5, customers must pay minimum demand charges designed to improve cost-causation alignment. However, the JLARC report found that data center load growth will likely increase system costs for all customers, including residential ratepayers \citep{JLARC2024, DeCarolis2025}.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:literature}
% ==========================================================================

\subsection{Quasi-Experimental Evidence on Infrastructure Disamenities}

The hedonic price function \citep{Rosen1974} provides the theoretical foundation for valuing local disamenities through housing markets. \citet{Kuminoff2013} argue that quasi-experimental designs offer more credible identification than cross-sectional hedonics.

\citet{Davis2011} finds 3--7 percent housing value declines within 2 miles of new power plants using a DiD design. \citet{Currie2015} study 1,600 toxic plant openings and closings, finding 11 percent declines within 0.5 miles. \citet{Muehlenbachs2015} demonstrate heterogeneous effects from shale gas, and \citet{LindenRockoff2008} show sharp spatial decay of disamenity capitalization. In the energy context, \citet{Gibbons2015} estimates negative visual disamenity effects from wind turbines, while \citet{Hoen2015} find no significant US effect. \citet{Jarvis2025} reconciles these with distance-ring DiD, and \citet{Bollinger2024} show transmission lines---a direct consequence of data center load---reduce values within 0.5 km by 10 percent. \citet{KielMcClain1995} track responses across the siting lifecycle (rumor, construction, operation), highlighting the importance of treatment timing---a key issue for this paper.

Data centers have received almost no attention. \citet{Waters2025} find no cross-sectional association in 2023 Northern Virginia sales. \citet{Hogan2025} document noise, water, and aesthetic complaints from Northern Virginia residents, but do not estimate property value effects. This paper provides the first quasi-experimental estimate.


\subsection{Identification in Staggered Treatment Settings}

\citet{GoodmanBacon2021} shows TWFE DiD decomposes into weighted 2$\times$2 comparisons, some using already-treated units as controls. \citet{deChaisemartin2020} demonstrate negative-weighted ATT estimates. \citet{SunAbraham2021} propose an interaction-weighted estimator that is robust to heterogeneous treatment effects; \citet{CallawaySantAnna2021} develop group-time ATTs; and \citet{Borusyak2024} propose imputation-based estimation. \citet{Roth2023} synthesize the literature with practical guidance.

Following \citet{Roth2023}, I report both the \citet{SunAbraham2021} estimator (which addresses heterogeneity concerns) and standard TWFE (for comparability with the existing literature). Differences between the two are informative about the degree to which heterogeneous treatment effects across data center cohorts matter in this setting.


\subsection{Local Public Finance and Fiscal Zoning}

\citet{Greenstone2010} estimate agglomeration spillovers from large plant openings using runner-up counties as controls. \citet{Bartik2020} finds that approximately 75 percent of business incentives are inframarginal, and \citet{Slattery2020} document that average state subsidies amount to \$178 million per 1,500 promised jobs. \citet{Jaros2025} examines data center tax abatements specifically, finding that abatements function as long-term contracts with ambiguous fiscal effects.

Loudoun County's situation is distinctive because it captures BPP revenue without offering local abatements---unlike the national pattern documented in these studies. The fiscal question is not whether incentives ``work'' but whether the county's capture of data center tax revenue creates a systematic incentive to under-regulate localized harms.


\subsection{Legal Regime Choice: By-Right vs.\ Discretionary Siting}

The choice between by-right (as-of-right) and discretionary (special exception/conditional use) approval is a foundational question in land use law. \citet{CalabresiMelamed1972} provide the canonical framework: property rules protect entitlements with injunctions (high transaction costs to overcome), while liability rules protect with damages (lower transaction costs, but potential undercompensation). \citet{Fischel2001} argues that homeowners act as ``homevoters'' whose political engagement is driven by property value protection. \citet{Dear1992} and \citet{Schively2007} review the NIMBY literature on community opposition to locally unwanted land uses.

By-right zoning assigns a de facto property rule to the developer: the right to build is established by the zoning code, and neighbors must organize politically to change the code---a high-cost, uncertain process. Discretionary review shifts the entitlement: the developer must affirmatively demonstrate that the project satisfies conditions, and affected parties can participate in hearings. The theoretical trade-off is between the certainty and low transaction costs of by-right approval and the screening and internalization benefits of discretionary review.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
% ==========================================================================

\subsection{Property Transactions}

I construct a panel of residential property transactions from the Loudoun County Commissioner of Revenue's publicly available Real Property Sales Reports for 2020--2025. I retain arm's-length residential resales (sale verification code ``1:MARKET SALE''), excluding new construction, multi-parcel transactions, foreclosures, and commercial properties. New construction sales (24 percent of the raw sample) are excluded because builder pricing reflects cost-plus margins rather than hedonic capitalization of neighborhood amenities. This yields 31,333 usable resale transactions.

Structural characteristics come from the 2026 Residential Dwelling Data file, providing living area, bedrooms, bathrooms, year built, grade, and condition. Because these characteristics are measured from a single cross-section (2026 assessments) rather than at each transaction date, they may not reflect the property's condition at the time of sale. In the parcel fixed effects specification, this concern is mitigated because time-invariant structural characteristics are absorbed. In the tract fixed effects specifications, any post-sale renovations correlated with data center proximity could bias results; the likely direction is attenuation, since proximity to industrial uses may discourage investment.


\subsection{Data Center Inventory and Treatment Timing}

I construct a data center inventory from Loudoun County's official ArcGIS portal. The inventory contains 135 data center parcels, of which 107 are classified as built with a known permit date. Building permit dates are available for 130 of 135 parcels. The median lag between permit issuance and facility opening is approximately one year.

I use the \emph{permit year} rather than the opening year as the primary treatment date. This choice is motivated by the siting-lifecycle literature \citep{KielMcClain1995, Dehring2007}: housing markets typically respond to observable signals of future development---zoning applications, permit issuance, and construction activity---rather than waiting for facility completion. Using opening year risks mis-timing treatment, generating spurious ``pre-trends'' that actually reflect post-permit adjustment and attenuating post-treatment estimates. I report opening-year results as a robustness check.

\subsection{Exposure Measures}

For each transaction, I compute two types of exposure. The \emph{nearest-facility} measure assigns each property to a distance ring (0--1 km, 1--2 km, 2--4 km, or $>$4 km) based on distance to the nearest permitted data center. The \emph{cumulative exposure} measure computes the total data center square footage and count of facilities within 2 km and 4 km of each property, counting only facilities permitted before the sale date. This time-varying cumulative measure addresses the concern that nearest-facility distance ignores the density and scale of nearby development: in Loudoun, 90 percent of data centers cluster with three or more neighbors within 2 km.

\subsection{Fiscal and Electricity Data}

Tax revenue data come from Board of Supervisors FGOEDC presentations (FY2015--FY2022 actuals), the JLARC 2024 report (FY2023), and county sources (FY2024). Electricity data come from EIA Form 861 and the JLARC report's infrastructure cost estimates.

\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:strategy}
% ==========================================================================

\subsection{Hedonic Framework}

Following \citet{Rosen1974}, I model log sale prices as a function of structural characteristics, location, proximity to data centers, and treatment timing:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hedonic}
\log(p_{it}) = \sum_{r} \beta_r \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{Ring}_{ir}] \cdot \text{Post}_{it} + \mathbf{X}_{it}'\boldsymbol{\gamma} + \alpha_j + \tau_t + \varepsilon_{it}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\text{Post}_{it} = \mathbf{1}[\text{sale year} \geq \text{permit year of nearest DC}]$; $\mathbf{X}_{it}$ includes living area, bedrooms, bathrooms, age, age squared, construction grade, and property type; $\alpha_j$ is a census tract fixed effect (74 tracts); $\tau_t$ is a year-quarter fixed effect; and the reference category is properties beyond 4 km from any data center. Standard errors are clustered at the census tract level.


\subsection{Sun-Abraham Estimator}

To address concerns about heterogeneous treatment effects in staggered settings \citep{GoodmanBacon2021, Roth2023}, I estimate the \citet{SunAbraham2021} interaction-weighted estimator as the primary specification. This defines cohorts by the permit year of each property's nearest data center and estimates cohort-specific treatment effects that are then aggregated into a single ATT. Never-treated properties (beyond 4 km) serve as the comparison group. The Sun-Abraham estimator is robust to heterogeneous treatment effects across cohorts and does not suffer from the negative-weighting problems of standard TWFE.


\subsection{Event Study}

I estimate event study specifications indexed by years relative to the nearest data center's permit date, with $k = -1$ as the reference category and a window of $[-4, +4]$. Pre-treatment coefficients test the parallel trends assumption. Post-treatment coefficients trace the dynamic path of any disamenity capitalization.


\subsection{Anticipation and Timing}

A distinctive feature of data center development is that it is highly visible before completion: permit applications, site grading, and construction of large industrial buildings generate observable signals over a 12--24 month period. If housing markets capitalize expected disamenities at permit issuance or construction start rather than at facility opening, then an event study centered on opening year will underestimate the true effect (attenuation bias) and may generate spurious ``pre-trends'' that actually reflect post-knowledge adjustment. Using permit date as the treatment date partially addresses this concern, though earlier signals---such as zoning applications or transmission line proposals---may precede even the permit date.


\subsection{Identification Threats}

The primary threat is spatial sorting: if lower-quality homes systematically locate near industrial zones where data centers are built, the estimated effect captures pre-existing level differences rather than causal impacts. Census tract fixed effects address time-invariant spatial sorting. The repeat-sales specification with parcel fixed effects absorbs all time-invariant property heterogeneity, though it relies on a smaller and potentially selected subsample.

A second concern is the treatment of control properties. Properties beyond 4 km may still be affected by data center development through transmission infrastructure, traffic, or county-wide fiscal effects. To the extent that controls are contaminated, the estimated treatment effects are attenuated toward zero.

The magnitude is modest compared to power plants ($-3$ to $-7$ percent, \citealp{Davis2011}) or toxic facilities ($-11$ percent, \citealp{Currie2015}), and closer to the null or small effects found for wind farms \citep{Hoen2015}. This is consistent with data centers being a relatively contained disamenity (noise and visual impacts without air or water pollution).


\subsection{TWFE DiD with Distance Rings}

The TWFE regression table presents results using permit-date treatment timing on the resale-only sample. The ring$\times$post coefficients are negative across all specifications. The 1--2 km ring is the most precisely estimated: $-3.1$ percent ($p = 0.005$) in the tract FE specification and $-3.3$ percent ($p < 0.001$) in the repeat-sales parcel FE specification. The 2--4 km ring is also significant in both specifications. The 0--1 km ring is negative but imprecisely estimated, likely reflecting the thin sample at very close range (2,455 resale transactions within 1 km).

At the median assessed value of \$738,730, the 1--2 km effect implies a price difference of approximately \$22,700--\$24,400.


\subsection{Cumulative Exposure}

The continuous cumulative exposure measure---total data center square footage within 2 km, expressed per million square feet---yields a coefficient near zero ($0.001$, SE $= 0.004$) and is statistically insignificant. The count-based measure (number of DCs within 2 km) is similarly null ($-0.0006$, SE $= 0.0014$). This null result does not necessarily contradict the ring-based findings: it may reflect that the cross-sectional variation in cumulative exposure is largely absorbed by tract fixed effects, since exposure is highly correlated with location in Loudoun's clustered geography.


\subsection{Repeat-Sales and Subsample Selection}

The repeat-sales parcel FE specification (Column 4 in the regression table) controls for all time-invariant property characteristics but relies on a subsample of 3,390 transactions from 1,649 parcels that sold at least twice during 2020--2025.

Table~\ref{tab:balance} compares repeat-sale and single-sale properties. Repeat-sale properties have somewhat lower sale prices and assessed values and are more likely to be located within 2 km of a data center, suggesting mild selection toward more frequently transacted properties in denser, closer-in neighborhoods. The results from this subsample should therefore be interpreted as local treatment effects for properties with higher turnover, not as estimates for the typical Loudoun homeowner.

\subsection{Heterogeneity}

I test for treatment effect heterogeneity along two dimensions. First, by facility size: the post-treatment effect near hyperscale facilities ($>$500,000 sq ft) does not significantly differ from the effect near smaller facilities. Second, by cohort timing: early-permitted (2019--2021) and later-permitted (2022+) cohorts show similar treatment effects. The absence of strong heterogeneity provides some support for the TWFE specifications alongside the Sun-Abraham estimates, consistent with the guidance in \citet{Roth2023}.

\section{Distributional Incidence and Legal Analysis}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ==========================================================================

\subsection{Tax Savings}

Data center tax revenue has enabled a residential property tax rate reduction from \$1.145 to \$0.805 per \$100 of assessed value between 2012 and 2025. For the median homeowner (assessed value: \$738,730), this implies annual savings of \$2,512 in 2025 relative to the 2012 rate. The NPV of cumulative observed savings over 2012--2025, discounted at 3 percent, is \$12,086.

This should be understood as an \emph{upper-bound} estimate. The 2012 rate is a strong counterfactual: Loudoun experienced population growth, rising assessments, and commercial development during this period that would likely have permitted some rate reduction even absent data centers. The actual causal contribution of data center revenue to rate reductions is unknown and would require a general equilibrium model of county fiscal policy that is beyond the scope of this paper.


\subsection{Electricity Costs}

Using a capital recovery factor applied to \$22 billion in data-center-attributable T\&D costs and a 29.4 percent residential load share, I estimate an annual per-customer cost of \$218. The JLARC range is \$168--\$444. This cost accrues to all 2.36 million Dominion residential customers across Virginia, not exclusively to Loudoun residents. The GS-5 rate class, effective 2027, partially addresses cost-causation by imposing minimum demand charges on large customers, but its long-run effectiveness depends on load growth assumptions that are actively contested.


\subsection{Property Value Effects}

The most robust finding across specifications is a negative association of approximately 1.3--3.3 percent for properties within 1--2 km of a newly permitted data center. This is a suggestive estimate, not a definitive causal finding: the event study does not show a clean monotonically negative post-treatment path, the cumulative exposure measure is null, and the 0--1 km ring is imprecise. At the median assessed value, the 1--2 km range implies a price difference of approximately \$10,000--\$24,000.

Unlike the tax and electricity channels, property value effects are spatially concentrated. This paper identifies 3,236 unique parcels within 1 km and 12,513 within 2 km of a data center that transacted during 2020--2025.


\subsection{Incidence Table}

Table~\ref{tab:incidence} presents the distributional incidence across channels. The key observation is that each channel affects a different population: tax savings accrue to Loudoun homeowners, electricity costs to Dominion customers statewide, and property value effects to proximate homeowners only. Aggregating these into a single ``net welfare'' figure would require assumptions about the relative standing of these populations and the commensurability of capitalized stock losses with ongoing flows that the data do not support.

\subsection{Legal Regime Choice: By-Right vs.\ Special Exception}
\label{sec:legal}

The distributional pattern documented above illuminates the legal question at the heart of Loudoun's 2025 zoning reform. Under the by-right regime, data center development was a permitted use in industrial zones. In the \citet{CalabresiMelamed1972} framework, this assigned a de facto property rule to the developer: the entitlement to build was established by the zoning code, and affected neighbors could not veto the project. The only recourse was political---lobbying to change the code itself, a high-transaction-cost activity that succeeded only after two decades of accumulating externalities.

The Special Exception regime shifts toward a liability-rule structure. The developer must affirmatively demonstrate that the project satisfies conditions, the community can participate in hearings, and the Board retains discretion to deny or condition approval. This lowers the transaction costs of internalizing localized harms but introduces new costs: regulatory uncertainty for developers, potential hold-up problems, and the risk that discretionary review becomes a mechanism for rent extraction rather than welfare-improving screening.

The fiscal externality problem is central to this analysis. Under the by-right regime, the county government captured substantial BPP tax revenue from each new data center while bearing minimal direct costs of the localized harms---noise, traffic, visual impacts, and infrastructure strain fell on nearby residents and utility ratepayers. This created a systematic incentive for the county to over-approve relative to the socially efficient level. The county was, in effect, a fiscal beneficiary with approval authority but without full cost internalization---a classic principal-agent misalignment.

The GS-5 rate class represents a partial solution on the electricity side: by requiring large customers to pay minimum demand charges, it shifts some infrastructure costs from residential ratepayers to the cost-causal party. No analogous mechanism existed for property value externalities under the by-right regime. The Special Exception process fills this gap by creating a forum in which localized harms can be weighed against fiscal benefits---though whether discretionary review actually improves welfare or merely reallocates bargaining power remains an empirical question that this paper cannot resolve.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ==========================================================================

This paper examines the distributional incidence of data center development in Loudoun County, Virginia through three channels. A Sun-Abraham cohort-robust estimator yields an average treatment effect of $-1.3$ percent on residential sale prices near newly permitted data centers, and TWFE ring specifications suggest effects of $-1.6$ to $-3.3$ percent at 1--4 km. The evidence is suggestive of localized disamenity capitalization, though the results are sensitive to specification and the magnitude is modest compared to heavier industrial disamenities. An upper-bound estimate of annual tax savings is \$2,512 per homeowner, and electricity rate increases are estimated at \$168--\$444 per residential customer.

The central insight is distributional: these channels affect different populations, and aggregating them into a net welfare figure requires assumptions the data do not support. What the evidence does support is a pattern consistent with the political economy of NIMBY opposition: diffuse fiscal benefits sustained political support for data center development for two decades, while spatially concentrated costs accumulated until they generated sufficient political pressure to change the legal entitlement structure.

Several limitations deserve emphasis. First, the 6-year panel (2020--2025) limits the event study window and precludes long-run effect estimation. Second, structural controls from a single 2026 cross-section may not reflect conditions at sale. Third, the nearest-facility exposure measure ignores cumulative exposure, which the continuous measure does not resolve given the geographic correlation with tract fixed effects. Fourth, the tax counterfactual is an upper bound. Fifth, the paper does not directly observe the mechanisms generating the property value effect---noise, visual impact, traffic, or stigma---and cannot distinguish among them.

Future research could address these limitations with a longer panel, direct measurement of noise and visual exposure, and analysis of the political process through which the Special Exception regime was adopted. As data centers proliferate nationally, the distributional trade-offs documented here---between fiscal windfalls and localized harms, between ratepayer costs and developer profits---will become increasingly important for siting policy.
