\title{Data Center Alley:
       Tax Incidence, Property Values, and the Political Economy
       of Infrastructure Siting in Loudoun County, Virginia}

\author{Christopher Nesseth}

\maketitle
\thispagestyle{empty}

\vfill

\begin{abstract}
\singlespacing
\noindent Loudoun County, Virginia hosts approximately 200 data centers generating over \$895 million in annual tax revenue (roughly one-third of the county's operating budget) and funding a 30~percent residential property tax rate reduction since 2012 that saves the median homeowner approximately \$2,512 per year. I examine the distributional incidence of this concentration through tax revenue, property values, and electricity rates. Using a \citet{SunAbraham2021} cohort-robust event study with 31,333 resale transactions (2020--2025) and staggered data center permit dates, I estimate an average treatment effect on the treated of $-1.3$ percent ($p < 0.01$), but the evidence does not support a stable, tightly identified disamenity effect. The 0--1~km ring is imprecisely estimated, the event study does not show a monotonically increasing post-treatment pattern, and a cumulative exposure measure is null at a 2~km radius but modestly negative at 4~km. Meanwhile, data-center-driven electricity infrastructure costs of \$168--\$444 per year per residential customer are borne statewide and are being addressed through rate design reform. The institutional setting separated who captured fiscal gains, who bore localized burdens, and who held approval rights. The housing evidence does not show localized losses robustly enough to clearly justify the 2025 shift from by-right development to discretionary Special Exception review, though the design is better at ruling out a strong harm narrative than at establishing a precise zero effect.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ==========================================================================

Loudoun County, Virginia is the largest data center market in the world. Its approximately 200 data center facilities generate an estimated \$895 million in annual tax revenue (roughly one-third of the county's entire operating budget) and have enabled the Board of Supervisors to reduce the residential real property tax rate every year for more than a decade, from \$1.145 per \$100 of assessed value in 2012 to \$0.805 in 2025. At the current median assessed value of \$738,730, this rate reduction saves the typical homeowner approximately \$2,512 per year. Loudoun's residential tax rate is now 28~percent below Fairfax County's (\$1.125) and 13~percent below Prince William County's (\$0.920), a gap likely reflecting in large part data center revenue. Yet on March 18, 2025, the Board of Supervisors voted to eliminate by-right data center development across the county, requiring each new facility to obtain a Special Exception through public hearings. Does the evidence support this regulatory shift?

This paper investigates the distributional incidence of data center development through three channels. First, I document the fiscal benefit channel, with annual tax savings of approximately \$2,512 per homeowner accruing to roughly 130,000 county households. Second, I use a hedonic difference-in-differences (DiD) design to estimate property value effects. Using 31,333 residential resale transactions from 2020 to 2025, I exploit staggered data center permit dates as treatment events and employ the \citet{SunAbraham2021} cohort-robust estimator. The Sun-Abraham average treatment effect on the treated (ATT) is $-1.3$ percent ($p < 0.01$), but the evidence is specification-sensitive. The closest distance ring (0--1~km) is imprecisely estimated, the event study does not show a monotonically increasing post-treatment pattern, and a cumulative exposure specification (which exploits continuous, time-varying treatment intensity rather than arbitrary distance cutoffs) is null at a 2~km radius but modestly negative at 4~km. Third, I estimate the electricity rate incidence. Data-center-driven transmission and distribution capital expenditure by Dominion Energy Virginia imposes an estimated \$168--\$444 per year on each residential customer across the utility's service territory \citep{JLARC2024}.

The weight of the evidence reveals a substantial asymmetry with large fiscal benefits that accrue to all county homeowners. The hedonic design does not find robust evidence of economically meaningful property value harm, and the cumulative exposure specification is radius-sensitive (null at 2~km, modestly negative at 4~km) rather than cleanly identified at any single radius. Electricity costs are real but diffuse, borne statewide, and being addressed through rate design reform. This incidence pattern (large diffuse benefits alongside weak evidence of localized harm) complicates a straightforward welfare justification for the 2025 reallocation of approval rights. The institutional arrangement separated who captured fiscal gains, who bore localized burdens, and who held approval authority. Whether the housing evidence is robust enough to clearly justify that reallocation of decision rights is the law-and-economics question this paper takes up.

The paper contributes to several literatures. It provides the first quasi-experimental estimate of data center proximity effects on residential property values, extending the infrastructure disamenity literature on power plants \citep{Davis2011}, toxic facilities \citep{Currie2015}, shale gas \citep{Muehlenbachs2015}, and wind turbines \citep{Gibbons2015, Jarvis2025}. It contributes to the fiscal impact literature \citep{Greenstone2010, Bartik2020, Slattery2020} by documenting the unusually large tax base contribution of data centers under Virginia's Business Personal Property (BPP) structure. And it connects the empirical evidence to the political economy of "not in my backyard" (NIMBY) opposition and infrastructure siting, building on \citet{Olson1965}, \citet{Fischel2001}, and \citet{GlaeserGyourko2018}.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
% ==========================================================================

The Ashburn corridor of Loudoun County, Virginia is the largest data center market in the world. The concentration traces to a single infrastructure decision. In 1992, the Metropolitan Area Ethernet exchange point MAE-East was established near Dulles International Airport, making Ashburn one of only four original internet exchange nodes in the United States. The low-latency advantage of co-location near this exchange attracted the first wave of internet companies in the mid-1990s. When the dot-com bubble burst in 2001, many facilities stood vacant, but the underlying fiber infrastructure remained, and the corridor proved durable. Recovery was gradual through the 2000s and then accelerated sharply as cloud computing scaled.

By the early 2010s, Ashburn had earned the informal designation "Data Center Alley.'' Today Loudoun County is home to approximately 200 data centers (more than any other jurisdiction on earth) and as of 2024 had permitted 43 million square feet of data center space, a 231 percent increase over five years. Northern Virginia as a whole had surpassed 4,900 megawatts (MW) of operational data center capacity by Q1 2025, with Loudoun County accounting for roughly half of the state's data centers by number of sites, square footage, and energy usage \citep{JLARC2024}. Hyperscale facilities (those operated by Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, and Meta) held approximately 53 percent of the Northern Virginia market share in 2025.

The fiscal transformation of Loudoun County is striking. The county levies a BPP tax on the computer equipment and servers housed in data centers. Because this equipment depreciates rapidly and turns over frequently, it generates large and growing annual assessments. The trajectory of data center tax revenues illustrates the county's fiscal dependence on the sector, growing from approximately \$146 million in FY2016 to \$423 million in FY2020, \$663 million in FY2022, \$733 million in FY2023, and \$875 million in FY2024 \citep{JLARC2024}. By FY2025, data centers contributed an estimated \$895 million in annual tax revenue, projected to exceed \$1.3 billion by FY2027. The Joint Legislative Audit and Review Commission (JLARC) report estimated that data centers contribute \$9.1 billion to Virginia GDP and support 74,000 jobs statewide annually.

This revenue has allowed the Board of Supervisors to reduce the residential real property tax rate every year for more than a decade, from \$1.145 per \$100 assessed value in tax year 2012 to \$0.805 in 2025. The resulting tax advantage relative to peer counties is substantial. Fairfax County's residential rate is \$1.125, Prince William County's is \$0.920, and Arlington County's is \$1.033. A Loudoun homeowner with a \$738,730 assessed value pays approximately \$2,500 less per year than a comparable Fairfax homeowner, a gap largely attributable to data center revenue. The fiscal productivity is exceptional, with data centers generating roughly 19 times the gross revenue per acre of residential land on approximately 1,600 acres. Unlike many other jurisdictions nationally \citep{Jaros2025}, Loudoun County does not provide locally negotiated tax abatements on BPP, though Virginia provides a state-level sales and use tax exemption on equipment purchases that forgoes roughly \$1 billion per year in state revenue \citep{JLARC2024, GoodJobsFirst2025}.

Data centers had historically been permitted by right in Loudoun County's industrial and planned development zones. The absence of a special-use or conditional-use permit requirement meant approvals were administrative, handled by staff without Board review or public hearings. The regulatory environment shifted decisively between 2023 and 2025 as community opposition intensified. A proposed overhead transmission line traversing farmlands, wetlands, and a historic village (necessitated by data center load growth) galvanized opposition groups. In 2024, the Board denied a major data center application for the first time. On March 18, 2025, the Board approved a Comprehensive Plan amendment eliminating by-right data center development. Data centers now require a Special Exception, a legislative review with public hearings. This shift in the approval regime provides the institutional setting for the political economy analysis in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

Northern Virginia data centers require approximately 4,140 MW of electricity \citep{JLARC2024}. Dominion Energy Virginia projected \$22 billion in data-center-driven transmission and distribution costs over 15 years. The State Corporation Commission approved a new GS-5 rate class in 2025, effective January 2027, covering customers demanding 25 MW or more. Under GS-5, customers must pay minimum demand charges designed to improve cost-causation alignment. However, the JLARC report found that data center load growth will likely increase system costs for all customers, including residential ratepayers \citep{JLARC2024, DeCarolis2025}.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:literature}
% ==========================================================================

The hedonic price function \citep{Rosen1974} provides the theoretical foundation for valuing local disamenities through housing markets. \citet{Kuminoff2013} argue that quasi-experimental designs offer more credible identification than cross-sectional hedonics.

\citet{Davis2011} finds 3--7 percent housing value declines within 2 miles of new power plants using a DiD design. \citet{Currie2015} study 1,600 toxic plant openings and closings, finding 11 percent declines within 0.5 miles. \citet{Muehlenbachs2015} demonstrate heterogeneous effects from shale gas, and \citet{LindenRockoff2008} show sharp spatial decay of disamenity capitalization. In the energy context, \citet{Gibbons2015} estimates negative visual disamenity effects from wind turbines, while \citet{Hoen2015} find no significant US effect. \citet{Jarvis2025} uses distance-ring DiD to study NIMBYism around renewable energy projects, and \citet{Bollinger2024} show transmission lines (a direct consequence of data center load) reduce values within 0.5 km by 10 percent. \citet{KielMcClain1995} track responses across the siting lifecycle (rumor, construction, operation), highlighting the importance of treatment timing, a key issue for this paper.

Data centers have received almost no attention. \citet{Waters2025} find no cross-sectional association in 2023 Northern Virginia sales. \citet{Hogan2025} document noise, water, and aesthetic complaints from Northern Virginia residents, but do not estimate property value effects. This paper provides the first quasi-experimental estimate.

\citet{GoodmanBacon2021} shows two-way fixed effects (TWFE) DiD decomposes into weighted 2$\times$2 comparisons, some using already-treated units as controls. \citet{deChaisemartin2020} demonstrate negative-weighted ATT estimates. \citet{SunAbraham2021} propose an interaction-weighted estimator that is robust to heterogeneous treatment effects. \citet{CallawaySantAnna2021} develop group-time ATTs, and \citet{Borusyak2024} propose imputation-based estimation. \citet{Roth2023} synthesize the literature with practical guidance.

Following \citet{Roth2023}, I report both the \citet{SunAbraham2021} estimator (which addresses heterogeneity concerns) and standard TWFE (for comparability with the existing literature). Differences between the two are informative about the degree to which heterogeneous treatment effects across data center cohorts matter in this setting.

\citet{Greenstone2010} estimate agglomeration spillovers from large plant openings using runner-up counties as controls. \citet{Bartik2020} finds that approximately 75 percent of business incentives are inframarginal, and \citet{Slattery2020} document that average state subsidies amount to \$178 million per 1,500 promised jobs. \citet{Jaros2025} examines data center tax abatements specifically, finding that abatements function as long-term contracts with ambiguous fiscal effects.

The Tiebout-Oates tradition establishes that local tax-service packages capitalize into property values \citep{Tiebout1956, Oates1969, Hamilton1976}, implying that fiscal benefits from data center revenue may partially offset disamenity capitalization in housing markets. Loudoun County's situation is distinctive because it captures BPP revenue without offering local abatements, unlike the national pattern documented in these studies. The fiscal question is not whether incentives "work'' but how the county's unusually large capture of data center tax revenue shapes the political economy of approval decisions.

\citet{Olson1965} provides the foundational theory of collective action asymmetries. Small groups with concentrated per-capita stakes organize more effectively than large groups with diffuse benefits, even when the large group's aggregate interest is greater. \citet{Fischel2001} argues that homeowners are "homevoters'' whose political engagement is disproportionately driven by property value threats. \citet{GlaeserGyourko2018} and \citet{MarbleNall2021} document how these dynamics generate systematic opposition to nearby development even when broader benefits are large. A consistent finding across infrastructure contexts is that opposition tends to be disproportionate to documented harms \citep{Jarvis2025, Davis2011, Dear1992, Schively2007}.

The choice between by-right and discretionary approval determines who holds the initial entitlement and the transaction costs of reallocation \citep{Coase1960, CalabresiMelamed1972}. By-right zoning assigns the entitlement to the developer, and opponents must organize politically to change the code. Discretionary review shifts effective veto power to the approval authority, lowering opposition costs but raising development costs. The deeper question is who captures the benefits and who bears the costs when the legal regime determines the default allocation of decision rights.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
% ==========================================================================

I construct a panel of residential property transactions from the Loudoun County Commissioner of Revenue's publicly available Real Property Sales Reports for 2020--2025. I retain residential resales excluding new construction, multi-parcel transactions, foreclosures, and commercial properties. New construction sales (24 percent of the raw sample) are excluded because builder pricing reflects cost-plus margins rather than hedonic capitalization of neighborhood amenities. This yields 31,333 usable resale transactions.

Structural characteristics come from the 2026 Residential Dwelling Data file, providing living area, bedrooms, bathrooms, year built, grade, and condition. Because these characteristics are measured from a single cross-section (2026 assessments) rather than at each transaction date, they may not reflect the property's condition at the time of sale. In the parcel fixed effects specification, time-invariant structural characteristics are absorbed, partially mitigating this concern. In the tract fixed effects specifications (which constitute the main design), post-sale renovations or deterioration correlated with data center proximity could bias results in either direction. The likely direction is attenuation if proximity to industrial uses discourages investment, but the direction cannot be established with certainty from the available data.

I construct a data center inventory from Loudoun County's official ArcGIS portal. The inventory contains 135 data center parcels, of which 107 are classified as built with a known permit date. Building permit dates are available for 130 of 135 parcels. The median lag between permit issuance and facility opening is approximately one year.

I use permit year rather than the opening year as the primary treatment date motivated by the siting-lifecycle literature \citep{KielMcClain1995, Dehring2007}. Housing markets typically respond to observable signals of future development (zoning applications, permit issuance, and construction activity) rather than waiting for facility completion. Using opening year risks mistiming treatment, generating spurious pre-trends that actually reflect post-permit adjustment and attenuating post-treatment estimates. Opening-year results are reported in Appendix~\ref{sec:appx_opening}.

For each transaction, I compute two types of exposure. The nearest facility measure assigns each property to a distance ring (0--1 km, 1--2 km, 2--4 km, or $>$4 km) based on distance to the nearest permitted data center. The cumulative exposure measure computes the total data center square footage and count of facilities within 2 km and 4 km of each property, counting only facilities permitted before the sale date. This time varying cumulative measure addresses the concern that nearest facility distance ignores the density and scale of nearby development. In Loudoun, 90 percent of data centers cluster with three or more neighbors within 2 km.

Tax revenue data come from Board of Supervisors Finance, Government Operations and Economic Development Committee presentations (FY2015--FY2022 actuals), the JLARC 2024 report (FY2023), and county sources (FY2024). Electricity data come from Energy Information Administration Form 861 and the JLARC report's infrastructure cost estimates.

Table~\ref{tab:descriptive} reports summary statistics. Figure~\ref{fig:map} shows the geographic distribution of data center facilities and residential transactions across Loudoun County. Data centers form several distinct clusters (Ashburn being the largest but not the only one), and each residential transaction is colored by its distance to the nearest of the 101 facilities. The figure makes visible both the clustered DC geography on which the identification in Section~\ref{sec:strategy} relies and the thinness of the 0--1~km ring relative to 1--2~km and 2--4~km, a direct consequence of industrial and planned-development zoning buffering the parcels immediately adjacent to each facility.

\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:strategy}
% ==========================================================================

Following \citet{Rosen1974}, I model log sale prices as a function of structural characteristics, location, proximity to data centers, and treatment timing:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hedonic}
\log(p_{it}) = \sum_{r} \beta_r \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{Ring}_{ir}] \cdot \text{Post}_{it} + \mathbf{X}_{it}'\boldsymbol{\gamma} + \alpha_j + \tau_t + \varepsilon_{it}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\text{Post}_{it} = \mathbf{1}[\text{sale year} \geq \text{permit year of nearest DC}]$; $\mathbf{X}_{it}$ includes living area, bedrooms, bathrooms, age, age squared, construction grade, and property type; $\alpha_j$ is a census tract fixed effect (74 tracts); $\tau_t$ is a year-quarter fixed effect; and the reference category is properties beyond 4 km from any data center. Standard errors are clustered at the census tract level.

To address concerns about heterogeneous treatment effects in staggered settings \citep{GoodmanBacon2021, Roth2023}, I estimate the \citet{SunAbraham2021} interaction-weighted estimator as the primary specification. This defines cohorts by the permit year of each property's nearest data center and estimates cohort-specific treatment effects that are then aggregated into a single ATT. Never-treated properties (beyond 4 km) serve as the comparison group. The Sun-Abraham estimator is robust to heterogeneous treatment effects across cohorts and does not suffer from the negative-weighting problems of standard TWFE.

I estimate event study specifications indexed by years relative to the nearest data center's permit date, with $k = -1$ as the reference category and a window of $[-4, +4]$. Pre-treatment coefficients test the parallel trends assumption. Post-treatment coefficients trace the dynamic path of any disamenity capitalization.

A distinctive feature of data center development is that it is highly visible before completion. Permit applications, site grading, and construction of large industrial buildings generate observable signals over a 12--24 month period. If housing markets capitalize expected disamenities at permit issuance or construction start rather than at facility opening, then an event study centered on opening year will underestimate the true effect (attenuation bias) and may generate spurious "pre-trends'' that actually reflect post-knowledge adjustment. Using permit date as the treatment date narrows timing error relative to opening year, but does not eliminate anticipation. For projects like data centers, information likely arrives before permit issuance through zoning applications, site assembly, grading activity, transmission line controversy, and construction visibility. This design reduces the risk that treatment timing is misspecified.

The primary threat is spatial sorting. If lower-quality homes systematically locate near industrial zones where data centers are built, the estimated effect captures pre-existing level differences rather than causal impacts. Census tract fixed effects address time invariant spatial sorting. The repeat sales specification with parcel fixed effects absorbs all time invariant property heterogeneity, though it relies on a smaller and potentially selected subsample.

A second concern is control group contamination. Properties beyond 4 km serve as never treated comparisons, but they may still be affected by data center development through transmission infrastructure, traffic patterns, and county wide fiscal changes. To the extent that control properties benefit from the tax savings funded by data center revenue, the treatment-control contrast understates the true net effect of proximity. This contamination likely attenuates point estimates toward zero, but it also means the "never-treated'' label is an approximation rather than a strict condition.

A notable feature of the results is that the 0--1~km ring is imprecisely estimated while the 1--2~km ring yields the strongest effects. For a genuine localized disamenity, one would expect the closest ring to show the largest effect with monotonic spatial decay. Three factors may explain this anomaly. First, only 2,455 resale transactions occurred within 1~km, limiting statistical power. Second, data centers in Loudoun are overwhelmingly located in industrial and planned development zones, where commercial and industrial parcels typically buffer the nearest residential properties, attenuating close range residential effects. Third, at very close distances, small geocoding errors in parcel centroids produce larger proportional distance errors. This pattern raises questions about the ring based estimates that should temper confidence in their causal interpretation.

Figure~\ref{fig:sa} plots the cohort-robust event study coefficients using permit year treatment timing. Pre-treatment coefficients at event times $-3$ and $-2$ are small and not individually significant, but a joint Wald test rejects parallel pre-trends at the 5~percent level (TWFE $F(2,49)=3.23$, $p=0.048$; SA $F(2,50)=3.81$, $p=0.029$; Appendix Table~\ref{tab:appx_pretrends}). The magnitude is small compared to power plants ($-3$ to $-7$ percent, \citealp{Davis2011}) or toxic facilities ($-11$ percent, \citealp{Currie2015}), and comparable to the null or small effects found for wind farms \citep{Hoen2015}. This is consistent with data centers being a relatively contained disamenity (noise and visual impacts without air or water pollution). The post-treatment dynamic path is not cleanly monotonic (which one would expect from a disamenity that capitalizes into prices over time), which further weakens the causal interpretation.

Table~\ref{tab:main} presents the full regression results using permit date treatment timing on the resale only sample. Column (1) reports the TWFE ring specification with census tract fixed effects. The ring$\times$post coefficients are negative across all specifications. The 1--2 km ring is the most precisely estimated at $-3.1$ percent ($p = 0.005$) in the tract fixed effects specification (Column 1) and $-3.3$ percent ($p < 0.001$) in the repeat-sales parcel FE specification (Column 4). The 2--4 km ring is also significant in both specifications. The 0--1 km ring is negative but imprecisely estimated, likely reflecting the thin sample and buffering effects discussed in Section~\ref{sec:strategy}. At the median assessed value of \$738,730, the 1--2 km effect implies a price difference of approximately \$22,700--\$24,400.

Columns (2) and (3) report the continuous cumulative exposure specifications at a 2~km radius. The square-footage measure (total data center square footage within 2~km) yields a coefficient near zero ($0.001$, SE $= 0.004$). The count-based measure (number of DCs within 2~km) is similarly null ($-0.0006$, SE $= 0.0014$). At this radius, the cumulative exposure specification does not recover the effect the ring-based estimates suggest.

Extending the cumulative exposure radius to 4~km, however, produces modestly negative coefficients that are statistically distinguishable from zero (sqft: $-0.0044$, SE $=0.0017$; count: $-0.0018$, SE $=0.0007$; Appendix Table~\ref{tab:appx_cumul_4km}). At the median non-zero 4~km exposure of approximately 3 million square feet, this implies roughly a 1.3~percent price difference, consistent in magnitude with the SA ATT and with the 1--2~km and 2--4~km ring estimates. The 2~km cumulative null therefore does not represent a robust zero. It reflects that at a 2~km radius, cross-sectional variation in cumulative exposure is largely absorbed by tract fixed effects in Loudoun's clustered data center geography. Expanding to 4~km reintroduces within-tract variation that the fixed effects do not fully absorb, and the effect reappears.

The cumulative exposure results are consistent with the ring-based estimates rather than at odds with them, but the radius sensitivity is a useful reminder that even the cumulative specification does not yield a tight identification of a causal proximity effect. The repeat sales parcel fixed effects specification (Column 4 of Table~\ref{tab:main}) controls for all time-invariant property characteristics but relies on a subsample of 3,390 transactions from 1,649 parcels that sold at least twice during 2020--2025. Repeat-sale properties are somewhat selected toward lower-priced, more frequently transacted parcels (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:balance}), so the repeat-sales estimates are best read as local effects for this subsample rather than as estimates for the typical Loudoun homeowner.

Several additional checks assess the stability of the results, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:robustness}. A leave-one-tract-out jackknife finds no influential tracts, with the ATT ranging from $-0.0155$ to $-0.0119$ across 51 iterations. A placebo permutation test shuffling treatment dates 500 times places the actual ATT at the 33rd percentile of the placebo distribution ($p = 0.334$), a result not distinguishable from randomly assigned treatment timing. A \citet{CallawaySantAnna2021} group time ATT yields $-6.0$ percent, more than four times the Sun-Abraham estimate, though with estimation difficulties for the 2024 cohort. Alternative distance cutoffs (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:alt_rings}) show the 1--2~km ring statistically significant at $p = 0.004$, with the 0--0.5~km and 0.5--1~km rings insignificant. Treatment effect heterogeneity is minimal along both facility size (Large DC minus Small DC difference $=0.009$, $p=0.59$) and cohort timing dimensions (Early minus Late difference $=0.018$, $p=0.39$; Appendix Table~\ref{tab:appx_heterogeneity}), providing some support for the TWFE specifications \citep{Roth2023}. Taken together, the point estimate is stable to sample perturbations but not to treatment-timing perturbations or alternative estimation strategies. These checks are the reason the paper's claim about the housing evidence is narrow. The design does not find robust evidence of economically meaningful localized harm. Figure~\ref{fig:placebo} illustrates the placebo distribution.

To clarify the hierarchy of credibility, I regard the cumulative exposure and ring-based specifications as mutually informative rather than as one dominating the other. The cumulative exposure specification does not rely on arbitrary distance cutoffs, exploits continuous time-varying treatment intensity, and better fits Loudoun's clustered data center geography; but it is radius-sensitive, null at 2~km and modestly negative at 4~km. The ring-based specifications are useful for comparison with the existing literature and for illustrating spatial gradients, but their 0--1~km estimate is imprecise and the pattern is not cleanly monotonic. Taken together, the estimates point toward modest negative effects in the 1--4~km range with fragile identification at any single radius.

\section{Distributional Incidence and Political Economy}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ==========================================================================

Data center tax revenue has enabled a residential property tax rate reduction from \$1.145 to \$0.805 per \$100 of assessed value between 2012 and 2025. For the median homeowner (assessed at \$738,730), this implies annual savings of \$2,512 in 2025 relative to the 2012 rate. The NPV of cumulative observed savings over 2012--2025, discounted at 3 percent, is \$12,086.

This should be understood as an upper bound estimate. Loudoun experienced population growth, rising assessments, and commercial development during this period that would likely have permitted some rate reduction even absent data centers. However, the cross-county comparison provides corroborating evidence. Neighboring Fairfax County, which lacks a comparable data center tax base, maintained a rate of \$1.125 in 2025, 40 percent above Loudoun's. Prince William County's rate was \$0.920 and Arlington County's was \$1.033. While these counties differ from Loudoun in population, commercial composition, and budget priorities, the magnitude of the rate gap is consistent with data center revenue as a primary driver of Loudoun's unusually low residential tax burden.

Using a capital recovery factor applied to \$22 billion in data-center-attributable transmission and distribution costs and a 29.4 percent residential load share, I estimate an annual per-customer cost of \$218. The JLARC range is \$168--\$444. This cost accrues to all 2.36 million Dominion residential customers across Virginia, not exclusively to Loudoun residents. The GS-5 rate class, effective 2027, addresses cost-causation by imposing minimum demand charges on large customers. Rate design reform through the State Corporation Commission (which has jurisdiction over the affected ratepayer population) is a more directly targeted response to cross-jurisdictional cost spillovers than local zoning restrictions, which cannot reach the statewide ratepayer base.

The hedonic evidence does not support a stable, tightly identified localized disamenity effect. The Sun-Abraham ATT of $-1.3$ percent is statistically significant but economically modest, implying a price difference of approximately \$9,600 at the median assessed value. The TWFE ring specifications find larger effects at 1--2~km ($-3.1$ percent) and 2--4~km ($-1.6$ percent), but the closest ring (0--1~km) is imprecisely estimated, an anomalous pattern for a genuine localized disamenity. The event study does not show a monotonically increasing post-treatment path. The cumulative exposure specification is null at 2~km but modestly negative at 4~km, consistent in magnitude with the ring estimates; radius sensitivity prevents it from delivering the tight identification its continuous form might otherwise offer.

This evidence does not support a finding of robust, economically meaningful localized harm. The point estimates are in the small-to-modest range and roughly consistent across specifications, but no single specification cleanly identifies a causal effect, and the results as a whole are more informative about what the data rules out than about the precise size of any true effect. The design is better at ruling out a strong harm narrative than at establishing a precise zero with confidence.

Table~\ref{tab:incidence} presents the distributional incidence across channels. Tax savings of \$2,512 per year accrue to all county homeowners, electricity costs of \$168--\$444 per year are spread across 2.36 million statewide ratepayers, and property value effects (to the extent they exist) are concentrated among proximate parcels within 4~km. For homeowners beyond 4~km (the control group in the hedonic design), the evidence shows only fiscal benefits and modest statewide electricity costs.

\label{sec:legal}

The incidence pattern documented above supports a sequential political economy account of Loudoun's regulatory transition. Under the by-right regime, the county government captured substantial BPP tax revenue from each new data center while bearing minimal direct costs of localized harms. Noise, visual impacts, and infrastructure strain fell on nearby residents, and electricity infrastructure costs spilled over to Dominion's statewide ratepayer base. This structural arrangement produced the well-known pattern in which the fiscal decisionmaker does not fully internalize the localized physical costs of the activity it authorizes \citep{CalabresiMelamed1972}. The claim here is not that by-right approvals were welfare-incorrect. That judgment would require knowledge of the localized harm curve and of the marginal projects the regime admitted. The claim is narrower, that the institutional structure decoupled fiscal benefits from the residents who bore the nearest physical burdens.

As visible conflicts accumulated, nearby residents acquired political leverage and changed the institutional rules. The distributional pattern that enabled this shift maps onto \citet{Olson1965}'s theory of collective action. The concentrated opponents (approximately 3,000--12,500 households within 1--2~km) faced large perceived per-capita stakes and low coordination costs. Neighborhood associations, county meetings, and the politically salient transmission line controversy provided natural organizing mechanisms. The diffuse beneficiaries each receive \$2,512 per year in tax savings, but these savings are not naturally attributed to data centers and generate weak incentives for counter mobilization. The standard \citet{Olson1965} prediction (that the small concentrated group prevails) is consistent with what occurred. The \citet{Fischel2001} homevoter hypothesis cuts both ways in this case since the median Loudoun homeowner is a net fiscal beneficiary. That a geographically concentrated minority of homeowners appears to have helped shift the regime despite the majority's diffuse fiscal stake is the asymmetry \citet{Olson1965} predicts. This describes the collective-action structure of the shift, not its welfare consequences.

The 2025 shift to Special Exception changes who holds effective veto power. Under by-right development, opponents bore the burden of organizing politically to change the zoning code, a high transaction cost activity that succeeded only after two decades. Under the Special Exception regime, each project must survive public hearings and Board scrutiny, substantially lowering the cost of opposition while raising the cost of development. Whether this reallocation of decision rights improves welfare depends on whether the localized harms that discretionary review screens for are economically meaningful enough to justify the transaction costs of discretionary review. The housing evidence does not show large localized losses robustly enough to clearly justify the reallocation, though it also cannot establish that localized harms are zero.

The regime change operates against the backdrop of substantial fiscal dependence. Data center tax revenue funds roughly one-third of the county budget, so any slowdown in data center growth would tighten the county's budget constraint and could require residential tax rate adjustments. Any such costs would fall on the same diffuse majority that currently receives the tax savings funded by the sector. The magnitude of this channel depends on parameters this paper does not estimate, including the approval-probability differential between the two regimes and the elasticity of data center siting to approval risk.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ==========================================================================

This paper examines the distributional incidence of data center development in Loudoun County through three channels. The fiscal benefits are large (approximately \$2,512 per homeowner per year). The hedonic evidence for localized property value harm is fragile, with point estimates roughly consistent across specifications but no single specification cleanly identifying a causal effect. Electricity costs are diffuse, statewide, and being addressed through rate design reform. The institutional setting separated who captured fiscal gains, who bore localized burdens, and who held approval rights, in a manner that \citet{Olson1965} and \citet{Fischel2001} help explain. The housing evidence does not show localized losses robustly enough to clearly justify the 2025 shift from by-right development to discretionary Special Exception review, though the design is better at ruling out a strong harm narrative than at establishing a precise zero effect. This is a narrow law-and-economics claim about whether the evidence justifies the reallocation, not a broader welfare judgment about whether the Board should have acted differently.

Several limitations deserve emphasis. First, the 6-year panel (2020--2025) limits the event study window and precludes long-run effect estimation. Second, structural controls from a single 2026 cross-section are a genuine weakness and may not reflect conditions at sale, and this concern is only partially mitigated in the repeat-sales specification. Third, permit year narrows but does not eliminate treatment timing error, as market information likely arrives through earlier signals. Fourth, the control group beyond 4~km may be contaminated by county-wide fiscal effects and transmission infrastructure. Fifth, the tax savings counterfactual is an upper bound. Sixth, the paper does not directly observe the mechanisms generating the property value effect (noise, visual impact, traffic, or stigma) and cannot distinguish among them. Finally, I do not observe proposed and denied projects, and therefore cannot directly test whether approval decisions systematically overweighted fiscal benefits relative to local harms. The political economy argument rests on the incidence pattern rather than on a direct test of approval behavior.

Future research could address these limitations with a longer panel, direct measurement of noise and visual exposure, and project-level analysis of approval decisions and hearing records. The broader lesson of this paper is that incidence and institutional design cannot be separated in local infrastructure politics. Who captures the fiscal benefits of development, who bears the localized costs, and who holds effective veto power over the approval process are not independent questions. They jointly determine whether infrastructure is sited, and for whose benefit. As data centers proliferate nationally, these distributional questions will become increasingly important for siting policy.
